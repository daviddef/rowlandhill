# Aruba

**Date:** 19 July 2026 · **Purpose:** base-catalogue skeleton for Rowland — what a country-keyed
search for "Aruba" will and will not find, and where the pre-1986 material actually lives.

Scott volume before using it as a corpus-completeness benchmark.

This total is small **because Aruba's own issuing history is only 40 years old** (1986–2026) —
unlike most Rowland dossiers, there is no large classical-era tail to miss. The one real gap is
structural, not a counting error: **everything used on Aruba before 1 January 1986 is
catalogued under a different country's heading entirely** (see §3–§4).

## 2. First issue

**1 January 1986**, the day Aruba's *Status Aparte* (internal autonomy within the Kingdom of
the Netherlands) took effect. Two series were released simultaneously:

- A **definitive series** of local scenes/symbols — confirmed values include 5c (traditional
  house), 30c (snake), 60c (water skier) and 150c (watapana/divi-divi tree), designed by
  **Evelino Fingal**. Sources describe this series as being extended with further
  denominations later in 1986 (e.g. a 15c "traditional house" value on 5 February 1986) —
  **the exact original value count (4 vs. a larger set) is not consistently reported and is
  flagged in §9**.
- A **Status Aparte commemorative** of four stamps (25c, 45c, 55c, 100c) depicting the new
  constitution's symbols — flag, coat of arms, map of the island, and national anthem —
  designed by **Nigel Matthew**. One dealer listing (Europost) catalogues this specific set as
  **NVPH 1–4**, suggesting the commemorative, not the definitive, occupies the NVPH catalogue's
  opening numbers.

**Printer: Joh. Enschedé** (Royal Joh. Enschedé, Haarlem) — this is corroborated by multiple
independent sources (Post Aruba's own philatelic page description and third-party summaries)
and is consistent with Enschedé's role as the traditional printer of Dutch and Dutch-Caribbean
stamps since 1866, so it is asserted here as **verified**, not assumed.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers

The catalogue-relevant issuer names, in the form a cataloguer would actually search for:

- **Curaçao** — 1873–1948/49. Pre-dates Aruba's own stamps entirely; this is where *all*
  pre-1948 Aruba-used material sits.
- **Netherlands Antilles** / **Nederlandse Antillen** — 1948/49–2010 (as an issuer, it kept
  issuing long after Aruba departed). This is where *all* 1949–1985 Aruba-used material sits.
- **Aruba** — 1986–present. Not a successor of the 2010 Antilles dissolution.
- **2010 successors of the Netherlands Antilles** (for the graph, not for Aruba directly):
  **Curaçao** (again, as a new constituent country with its own new stamp series), **Sint
  Maarten**, and the **Caribbean Netherlands** (Bonaire/Sint Eustatius/Saba, using regular
  Netherlands stamps with local overprints/surcharges in some periods).

**The distinction the brief flagged as commonly muddled is real and worth stating plainly:
Aruba is a 1986 secession from the Netherlands Antilles, a full generation before the 2010
dissolution, and does not sit anywhere in the 2010 succession graph.** A succession edge for
Rowland should read `Netherlands Antilles → Aruba (1986)` as an entirely separate edge from
`Netherlands Antilles → {Curaçao, Sint Maarten, Caribbean Netherlands} (2010)`. Treating Aruba
as one of "the three 2010 successors" (a fairly common shorthand error, since people count
Aruba, Curaçao and Sint Maarten together as "the three countries of the Kingdom" without
noting the different exit dates) would misdate 24 years of Aruba's independent stamp history.

## 5. Inscriptions

- **`CURACAO`** (unaccented in the metal type of the era) — 1873 to 26 July 1949. Also seen
  written as `CURAÇAO` in later reference literature and dealer cataloguing, but the stamps
  themselves used the unaccented form.
- **`NED. ANTILLEN`** — abbreviated form used on definitive series from 1950.
- **`NEDERLANDSE ANTILLEN`** — full form, used especially on commemoratives.
- **`ARUBA`** — used on every Aruba-issued stamp since 1 January 1986.
- **Papiamento wording** — appears on Aruba-issued stamps as a subject/theme (e.g. the 14 July
  2023 issue marking 20 years of Papiamento as an official language, designed by Miriam De
  L'Isle), and Papiamento text appears within stamp designs and Post Aruba philatelic
  material, but **no source found in this pass documents Papiamento replacing Dutch/English as
  the primary printed inscription on a stamp** — treat any specific claim of a Papiamento-only
  inscribed stamp as unverified.

**The identification trap, with a concrete example:** pre-1986 Netherlands Antilles issues
regularly **depict** Aruban subjects while being inscribed `NEDERLANDSE ANTILLEN` or
`NED. ANTILLEN` — not `ARUBA`. A clean, cited example: the **1974 "50th Anniversary of the Oil
Industry in Aruba"** issue, subject "Lago Refinery at Night" (the Lago oil refinery was
Aruba's dominant 20th-century industry), catalogued as Netherlands Antilles — Michel AN 286 /
Yvert AN 474 / Stanley Gibbons AN 591 / NVPH AN 494. **A search keyed purely on the string
"Aruba" will not surface this stamp**, because the country field says Netherlands Antilles.
Any Rowland ingestion pipeline that tags stamps by inscription-string alone will silently lose
this entire category — subject-tagging (a `depicts` field distinct from `issuer`) is the fix.

## 6. Gaps

No confirmed multi-year gap was found in Aruba's own 1986–present issuing record — annual
counts vary widely (as low as 16 stamps in 2002, as high as 112 in 2011, per StampWorld's
year-range pages) but the program appears continuous. **This is "no gap found," not "no gap
exists"** — a year-by-year reconciliation against a primary catalogue was not done in this
pass. For the pre-1986 era, there is no "gap" in the country-succession sense: Aruba simply has
no independent stamp identity before 1986, by definition, not by omission (see §3–§4).

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

**Aruba is NOT a wallpaper issuer in the IGPC/Stamperija sense.** Every subject found in this
research — flora and fauna, the California Lighthouse, Papiamento, "Sunsets of Aruba," Lago
refinery anniversaries, the constitution and national symbols — is genuinely tied to Aruba.
No evidence surfaced of Aruba's post office issuing the generic, non-national fillers
(dinosaurs, unrelated royals, pop-culture tie-ins) that define the IGPC/Stamperija model, and
no UPU illegal-issue circular found in this pass names *Aruba's own postal administration* as
a violator.

That said, two honest caveats keep this from being a clean bill of health:

- **Agency involvement is real, and it draws the criticism the brief anticipated — just not
  the IGPC/Stamperija flavor of it.** Aruba's stamps (along with Curaçao, Sint Maarten and
  Suriname) are produced/marketed through **Van Reijen / IPA (International Philatelic
  Agencies Ltd., Guernsey)**, and separately sold through **WOPA+ (World Online Philatelic
  Agency)**, both legitimate distribution channels for genuine national postal
  administrations rather than name-licensing mills. Collector-community commentary
  (Stampboards forum discussion, not independently re-verified here) criticizes Van Reijen for
  "excessive and expensive stamp issues with high face values that don't represent actual
  postal rates" — a pricing/volume complaint, materially different from and less serious than
  an accusation of illegitimacy. The 112-stamp year (2011) is consistent with this kind of
  agency-driven volume.
- **Aruba appears to be a victim, not a perpetrator, of illegal stamps.** Multiple
  Philatelic-Webmasters-Organization (pwmo.org) pages list Aruba among Caribbean
  territories that have had **counterfeit/illegal stamps issued in their name by unauthorized
  third parties**, which Aruba's postal administration was (as of the source's writing)
  "in the process of organizing relevant denouncements to be published as UPU Circulars." This
  is the opposite problem from wallpaper: someone else counterfeiting Aruba's name, not Aruba
  diluting its own catalogue.

**Forgery risk in the classic Curaçao material (pre-1948)** is a real, separately-documented
concern in general philatelic literature (reprints and forged overprints are well known
across many 19th/early-20th-century colonial issues of this type), but no Aruba-specific or
Curaçao-specific forgery monograph was located and verified in this pass — flagged as
**uncertain**, not asserted.

## 8. Image sourcing

**Potentially the most important finding in this dossier, and one to get exactly right rather
than to oversell.**

- **The Netherlands' Auteurswet Article 15b** exempts works "made public by or on behalf of
  the public authority" from copyright infringement claims for further publication/reproduction,
  **unless copyright is expressly reserved** — by law/decree/regulation generally, or by a
  notice on the specific work or at its publication. The Dutch Supreme Court's 1987 "Beatrix
  stamp" ruling confirmed this applies broadly to reproduction and republication. **Dutch
  stamps 1852–1954/55 are treated as public domain on Wikimedia Commons on this basis**; stamps
  from 1955 onward require permission from both PostNL (successor to the state PTT, privatised
  1 January 1989) and the individual designer, because rights have in practice been reserved
  since privatisation.
- **Does this extend to Aruba?** Aruba is not merely "covered by the same rule" — it has its
  **own, separate copyright statute**: the *Landsverordening auteursrecht* (cited on Wikimedia
  Commons as *Landsbesluit van 23 oktober 2003 no. 9*). That statute contains its own
  public-authority exception, worded almost identically to Dutch Article 15b: "laws, decrees
  and regulations," "court sentences and administrative rulings," and **"any other work
  published by the public authorities"** are not protected **unless rights are expressly
  reserved** by law/regulation or by notice on the work or at publication (cited on Commons and
  on Wikipedia's *Copyright law of Aruba* summary as Article 11 or Article 12 depending on the
  source — the article number itself needs reconciling against the primary statute text before
  this is relied on). Aruba's general copyright term otherwise runs to 50 years post-mortem
  auctoris, with 50-years-from-publication for anonymous/organisational works and photographs.
- **The honest reading, not overstated:** this creates a *plausible* public-domain argument for
  Aruba-issued stamps under the "public authority work, rights not reserved" exception — but
  only if two conditions hold, and **neither was confirmed in this research pass**: (1) that
  Post Aruba (a government service until its **1 September 2005 privatisation** into Post
  Aruba N.V., a private company thereafter) still counts as "the public authority" for a given
  stamp's date, and (2) that copyright was **not** expressly reserved on that stamp — which, by
  direct analogy to the Dutch PTT/PostNL precedent above, is exactly the kind of reservation a
  privatised, commercially-agented postal operator (Van Reijen/IPA, WOPA+) would be expected to
  make once stamps became a recurring commercial product rather than a bare government
  notice. **Do not treat this as "Aruban stamps are public domain."** Treat it as: pre-2005,
  government-issued Aruba stamps have a real 15b-style argument that has not been tested here
  against an actual reservation notice; post-2005, commercially-agented Aruba stamps are the
  least likely stamps in this dossier's history to be unreserved.
- For pre-1986 material, the same logic applies one level up: Curaçao and Netherlands Antilles
  stamps (1873–1985) were issued by Dutch/Kingdom governmental postal services, so the
  Dutch-model 15b argument plausibly applies to them too, subject to the same "was it
  reserved" caveat and to Aruba/Netherlands Antilles copyright law's own history of adopting
  (or not adopting) the Dutch statute before 2003.
- **Practical sourcing:** Wikimedia Commons' `Category:Stamps of Aruba` could not be loaded in
  this pass (404 on the exact category name tried) — the correct category name/depth was not
  confirmed. Commons' `Commons:Copyright rules by territory/Aruba` page is the right reference
  page to consult before uploading any Aruba stamp image, and `Commons:Copyright rules by
  territory/Netherlands` is the right page for the pre-1986 Curaçao/Netherlands Antilles
  material.
- **Aruban banknotes are a documented counter-example, not a stamp fact but a useful
  calibration point:** Commons' Aruba copyright page states banknote copyright is explicitly
  held by the Central Bank of Aruba — i.e., that adjacent category of government-issued paper
  *has* had rights expressly retained. This shows the "unless reserved" carve-out is actively
  exercised by Aruban public bodies elsewhere, which is a reason for caution, not confidence,
  about the stamps.

## 9. Uncertain

- **The exact composition of the very first day's issue.** Post Aruba's own philatelic page
  and most secondary sources describe two 4-stamp sets (definitive + Status Aparte
  commemorative = 8 stamps) on 1 January 1986. StampData's listing instead groups **16**
  distinct subjects under a 1986-01-01 issue date, and other sources describe the definitive
  series being "extended" later in 1986 with additional values (e.g. a 15c value on 5 February
  1986). Whether the original definitive set was 4 values or a larger set released same-day,
  and whether StampData's 16 reflects the true first-day count or an aggregation of the whole
  early-1986 program, was **not resolved**.
- **Precise NVPH/Scott numbering for the first issues.** One dealer listing catalogues the
  Status Aparte commemorative as NVPH 1–4; the definitive series' NVPH/Scott numbers were not
  independently confirmed (a search surfaced Scott ranges "18/21" and "9/12" for later 1986
  extensions, but not a confirmed number for the original definitive values).
- **Which named catalogue's Aruba count is authoritative.** As detailed in §1, StampWorld
  (~1,158), StampData (~1,103), and Colnect's internal cross-references (~1,084–1,245
  depending on the sub-catalogue) disagree, and none was verified against a physical or
  authenticated-API catalogue source.
- **Whether "112 stamps in 2011" and "29 stamps in 2020" (StampWorld-derived) reconcile with
  WOPA+'s own listing of Aruba's 2020–2024 output (7, 3, 6, 5, 2)** — these numbers do not
  agree and the discrepancy was not resolved; it may reflect different counting conventions
  (souvenir sheets, se-tenant pairs, or revenue/official stamps included in one count and not
  the other).
- **The exact Aruban copyright-statute article number** for the public-authority exception —
  cited as Article 11 by one Commons page and Article 12 by Wikipedia's summary of the same
  Landsbesluit. Needs reconciling against the primary Aruban Landsverordening text.
- **Whether any Aruba-issued stamp has ever had copyright expressly reserved** — not confirmed
  either way; see §8.
- **Forgery literature specific to classic Curaçao or Aruba-used material** — general colonial
  philately is a known forgery risk area, but no Aruba/Curaçao-specific forgery reference was
  located and verified.
- **Wikimedia Commons' exact Aruba stamp category name and image count** — the category page
  tried in this pass 404'd; the correct name/location was not established.
