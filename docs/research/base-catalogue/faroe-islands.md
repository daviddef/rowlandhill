# Faroe Islands

Research dossier for base-catalogue scoping. Compiled 2026-07-19.

**Methodological note:** `WebFetch` was blocked by network policy in this environment, so all
findings below come from search-engine result summaries rather than from direct reading of the
source pages. Every claim is therefore second-hand. Claims I could not corroborate across at
least two independent-looking sources are marked and repeated in §9 (Uncertain). No date or
count below is invented; where I do not know, I say so.

---

## 1. Headline counts

A count is only meaningful with a named catalogue attached. Faroe Islands is unusual in that the
*numbering base* differs between catalogues by exactly six numbers, because Michel/Scott/AFA
place the 1919 and 1940–41 provisionals at the head of the Faroe Islands country list, while some
other treatments do not (see §4).

| Catalogue | Approx. total | Basis / date of count | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **StampWorld** (online) | **1,153** postage stamps, 1975–2026 | Stated on the StampWorld Faroe Islands country landing page | Medium-high — a directly stated figure, but StampWorld counts *stamps*, not major numbers, and its listing explicitly begins at 1975 (so it excludes the six provisionals) |
| **Colnect** (online) | **1,227** stamps | Stated on Colnect's Faroe Islands list | Medium — Colnect counts every collectable item (booklet singles, sheetlet components, ATM labels), so it systematically over-counts vs. a printed catalogue's major numbers |
| **Michel** | Numbering confirmed to start at **1** (1919 provisional), with 2–6 = 1940–41 provisionals and 7–20 = the 1975 first definitive series. A current highest number was **not** established. | Cross-referenced from dealer and forum listings citing "Michel 1", "Färöer 2–6", "Michel 7/20" | Numbering base: high. Total: **unverified** |
| **Scott** | Same numbering base: **Scott 1** = 1919 provisional, **Scott 2–6** = 1940–41 provisionals, **Scott 7–20** = 1975 first series. Highest current number not established. | Dealer listings citing "Scott 2–6" for the 1940/41 set of five, and "SC# 7-11, 13-20" for the 1975 set | Numbering base: high. Total: **unverified** |
| **FACIT** | Not established | — | **Unverified** |
| **AFA** (Danish; the de-facto standard for Denmark + Faroes + Greenland) | Not established, but AFA is referenced as the source for *printing quantities* of the 1940–41 provisionals | Forum reference to "AFA special catalogs" | **Unverified** |
| **Stanley Gibbons** | Not established. SG lists the Faroes in its Scandinavia volume (Part 11), **not** in the Commonwealth catalogue — see §4. | — | **Unverified** |

**Which count I trust, and why.** For a *working* head-count I would use **StampWorld's 1,153**,
because it is the only stated figure that maps reasonably onto major numbers and its 1975 start
point is transparent. Add **6** for the provisionals to get a Michel/Scott-comparable base of
roughly **1,160 major numbers** — that addition is *my inference*, not a sourced figure.

For a *philatelically authoritative* count, the right catalogue is **FACIT** (the Nordic
specialist) or **AFA** (Danish, and the one Faroese material is actually traded against in
Scandinavia). Both weigh more heavily than Scott or SG for this territory, because Scott and SG
both treat the Faroes as a minor peripheral listing while FACIT and AFA treat it as core
territory with full specialisation of the provisionals. **I was unable to obtain a total from
either.** That is a real gap and it means the numbers in this table should be treated as
provisional.

**Do not** cite the Colnect 1,227 as a major-number count. It is not one.

## 2. First issue

**Important correction to the framing of this research request.** The brief describes
30 January 1975 as "Postverk Føroya's own first issue." The date is right; the attribution is
**wrong**, and this appears to be a widely repeated conflation worth flagging.

| Field | Finding | Source status |
|---|---|---|
| **Date** | **30 January 1975** | Corroborated across multiple sources including the postal administration's own postal-history page. High confidence. |
| **Issuing authority on that date** | The **Danish** postal administration. Postverk Føroya did **not** exist yet. | Multiple sources state the Faroese government took over the postal service on **1 April 1976**, and that "in 1974–75 the Danish postal system began issuing Faroese postage stamps captioned FØROYAR." See §3 and the myth note below. |
| **Catalogue numbers** | **Scott 7–20** / **Michel 7–20** — fourteen values | Corroborated by dealer listings ("SC# 7-11, 13-20") and by the Michel "7/20" citation. High confidence on the range; note that a dealer listing offering 7-11 and 13-20 implies #12 was sold separately, not that it doesn't exist. |
| **Number of stamps** | **14** pictorial definitives | See myth note below. |
| **Subjects** | Pictorial definitives: roughly half depict **classical/historic maps** of the Faroes and the North Atlantic; the remainder are **island landscapes**. One documented high value, 500 øre, reproduces a painting of Hvítanes (Streymoy) with Skálafjørður (Eysturoy) beyond, after the Faroese painter **Sámal Joensen-Mikines** (1906–1979). | Medium-high. The map/landscape split and the Mikines value are stated in sources; I did not obtain a full 14-value checklist. |
| **Designer** | **Lydia Laksefoss** | Corroborated across several sources. High confidence. |
| **Engraver** | **Czesław Slania** | Corroborated, including by a Slania-specialist site and a Wikimedia Commons category "Słania's stamps for the Faroe Islands". High confidence. |
| **Perforation** | 12¾ | Single source. Medium confidence. |
| **Printer** | **NOT ESTABLISHED.** | I searched specifically for Courvoisier and for the Danish Royal Mint / Danish Post Office printing works and found **no confirmation of either**. Given Slania's engraving and recess production, the Danish Post Office stamp printing works in Copenhagen is the obvious candidate — but that is **my inference and is unverified**. Do not record a printer without checking a physical catalogue. |

### Myth flags for §2

- **MYTH: "Postverk Føroya issued the first Faroese stamps."** Widely repeated, including in
  dealer copy. Postverk Føroya was established **1 April 1976**, fourteen months *after* the
  30 January 1975 issue. The 1975 stamps were issued under Danish postal authority with a
  Faroese inscription. The correct catalogue-facing statement is: *first stamps inscribed
  FØROYAR, 30 January 1975, issued under the Danish postal administration.* Confusingly,
  the 1975 series is nonetheless described in some sources as marking the "Introduction of the
  Financially Independent Postverk Føroya," which is a forward-looking description, not a
  statement about who issued it.
- **MYTH: "19 stamps were issued on 30 January 1975."** One search result asserted 19. This does
  not survive checking — the catalogue range 7–20 is fourteen numbers, and multiple other
  sources say fourteen. **14 is correct; 19 is wrong.** This looks like exactly the kind of
  fabricated detail that AI-generated dealer pages are introducing.
- **CAUTION: "Lydia Laksefoss was the printer."** One search summary garbled this. Laksefoss was
  the **designer**. There is no evidence she was a printer.

## 3. Issuing periods

| Era | Dates | Issuing authority | Stamps used |
|---|---|---|---|
| Danish period (pre-provisional) | **1870** – 1918 | Royal Danish Post (Det Kongelige Danske Postvæsen) | Ordinary Danish stamps, no overprint or surcharge |
| **1919 provisional** | January 1919 (exact day not established) | Tórshavn Post Office, acting locally under Danish postal authority | Local surcharge on a Danish stamp |
| Danish period (interwar) | 1919 – April 1940 | Royal Danish Post | Ordinary Danish stamps |
| **British-period provisionals** | **November 1940 – May 1941** (surcharges); British administration of the islands ran **12 April 1940 – 1945** | Tórshavn Post Office. **Postal affairs remained regulated by the Danish Post Office throughout** — the British administration prompted the surcharges but did not take over the post. | Local surcharges on Danish stamps |
| Post-war Danish period | 1941/45 – January 1975 | Royal Danish Post | Ordinary Danish stamps. The wartime overprints were formally **invalidated by a Postal Administration letter to the Tórshavn Post Office dated 2 July 1953**. |
| **Faroese-inscribed, Danish-issued** | **30 January 1975 – 31 March 1976** | **Danish postal administration**, issuing stamps inscribed FØROYAR | Faroe Islands' own stamps |
| **Postverk Føroya** | **1 April 1976 – September 2009** | Postverk Føroya (Post of the Faroes) | Faroe Islands' own stamps |
| **Posta** | **September 2009 – present** | Posta Faroe Islands / "Posta" | Faroe Islands' own stamps |

Note the awkward 14-month window in 1975–76. A catalogue that assigns an "authority" per era must
either (a) split 1975 from 1976, or (b) accept a small inaccuracy. Posta itself markets 2026 as
its **50th anniversary**, i.e. dating the institution from 1976, which supports the split.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers — and where the provisionals are catalogued

**This is the section with the highest practical stakes and I could only partly resolve it.**

### Predecessor
- **Denmark** — the exact catalogue-facing name. Danish stamps were used in the Faroes from
  **1870** until 1975, normally **without overprint or surcharge**. A catalogue's predecessor
  field for Faroe Islands should read simply `Denmark`.

### Successor
- None. Posta is the current issuer; the Faroe Islands remain an active, ongoing issuing entity.

### Where the 1919 provisional is catalogued

| Catalogue | Placement | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Michel** | Under **Färöer (Faroe Islands)** as **Michel 1** | A Numista/catalogue cross-reference explicitly gives "Michel: 1" for the 1919 provisional. High confidence. |
| **Scott** | Under **Faroe Islands** as **Scott 1** | Inferred from the fact that Scott 2–6 are confirmed as the 1940–41 set of five; #1 must therefore be the 1919 issue. **This is an inference, not a directly sourced statement.** |
| **StampWorld** | **Excluded** — StampWorld's Faroe Islands listing begins at 1975 | Stated on the site. High confidence. |
| **FACIT / AFA** | **Not established.** Danish and Nordic practice traditionally listed both the 1919 and the 1940–41 provisionals within the **Denmark** section as Faroese local issues, before Faroe Islands became a country listing in its own right. Whether current FACIT/AFA editions do this is **unverified**. | — |

### Where the 1940–41 provisionals are catalogued

| Catalogue | Placement | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Michel** | Under **Färöer** as **Michel 2–6** | Directly cited in a German philatelic forum thread ("Färöer 2-6"). High confidence. |
| **Scott** | Under **Faroe Islands** as **Scott 2–6** | Directly cited in a Danish auction lot description ("Kompletter Satz 5 Werte … Scott 2-6"). High confidence. |
| **Stanley Gibbons — Commonwealth catalogue** | **NOT LISTED, and deliberately so.** Because Faroese postal affairs continued to be regulated by the **Danish** Post Office throughout the British administration, these are not British Commonwealth occupation issues and do not qualify for inclusion. | Explicitly stated by a specialist source on British wartime occupations. High confidence, and this is a genuinely important distinction. |
| **Stanley Gibbons — Scandinavia (Part 11)** | Presumably listed here. **Unverified.** | — |
| **FACIT / AFA** | **Not established**, but AFA is cited as the reference for the **printing quantities** of these provisionals, which implies AFA lists them in specialised detail. | — |

**Practical consequence.** Anyone searching for these stamps under "British occupation issues"
in an SG Commonwealth framework will find nothing, and will wrongly conclude they are unlisted.
They are simply catalogued as **Faroe Islands** (Danish sphere), not as a British occupation.
This is a frequent point of confusion in dealer copy.

### The 1940–41 issues in detail

Trigger: on **10 July 1940** the Danish Post Office raised the standard inland rate from 15 to
20 øre; Denmark was under German occupation and the Faroes under British administration from
**12 April 1940**, so no new stamps could arrive. Tórshavn surcharged stock on hand. The base
stamps are cited as Danish **Michel 195, 202, 244 and 258**, surcharged in **black or blue**,
with the old value obliterated by multiple strikes.

Reported issue dates — **note the conflict**:

| Surcharge | Date (source A) | Date (source B) |
|---|---|---|
| 20 øre on 15 øre red | November 1940 | **2 November 1940** |
| 50 øre on 5 øre wine-red | November 1940 | **6 December 1940** |
| 60 øre on 6 øre orange-yellow | November 1940 | **21 December 1940** |
| 20 øre on 5 øre | March 1941 | 1941 |
| 20 øre on 1 øre | (implied 1941) | 1941 |

Source A compresses the 50/5 and 60/6 into "November 1940"; source B gives specific December
dates. **I trust the specific dates (source B) more** — precise dates are harder to fabricate
than a vague month, and source B's dates are internally consistent with a rolling series of
local surcharges. But this is unresolved: see §9.

A **second printing** of the 20/1, 50/5 and 60/6 was made in **May 1941**. Michel **2, 5 and 6
exist in versions A and B**, distinguished by the **spacing between the zeros** in the surcharge.
This A/B distinction is the single most important technical detail for identification and
valuation, and it is exactly what forgers target (§7).

## 5. Inscriptions

| Inscription | Period | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **DANMARK** | 1870 – 1975 | The stamps in use in the Faroes for a century carried only the Danish country name. The 1919 and 1940–41 provisionals are surcharged **DANMARK** stamps — they carry **no Faroese inscription at all**. This matters for OCR/inscription-matching: a Faroe Islands catalogue entry for Scott 1–6 will have an inscription that reads DANMARK. |
| **FØROYAR** | 1975 – present | The standard and, as far as I could establish, the **only** country inscription ever used on stamps issued for the Faroes. Multiple sources state the Danish postal system "began issuing Faroese stamps with the caption FØROYAR" in 1974–75. |
| **FÖROYAR** (o-diaeresis rather than o-slash) | — | **NOT VERIFIED as ever appearing on a stamp.** Ö is not a letter of the modern Faroese alphabet; Ø is. This spelling is most likely an artefact of German-language catalogues (Michel uses "Färöer" for the country name) or of character-set corruption in databases, **not** a printed inscription. I found no evidence of a stamp inscribed FÖROYAR. Treat as a **data-entry variant to normalise, not a genuine philatelic inscription** — but this is a negative finding from absence of evidence, so it is listed in §9. |
| Historic / vernacular forms | — | **None found.** Because there is no pre-1975 Faroese-inscribed stamp, there is no archaic inscription lineage of the kind found in, say, Iceland or Greenland. |
| **POSTVERK FØROYA** / **POSTA** | 1976– / 2009– | The *administration* names. Whether either appears as printed text on stamps (as opposed to on booklet covers, ATM labels and philatelic products) is **not established**. ATM/variable-value labels are the likeliest place to find POSTA printed. Unverified. |

Currency inscription throughout: **øre** and **krónur** (Faroese króna, at par with and
circulating alongside the Danish krone).

## 6. Gaps

**Two structural gaps, and I could not verify a third.**

1. **1870 – 1918** — no Faroese stamps. Not a "gap" in the usual sense; the Faroes simply had no
   separate stamp issue. Danish stamps served.
2. **1919 (after the provisional) – 1940** — no Faroese issues. The 1919 surcharge was a one-off
   emergency response to a rate change plus a shipping delay, not the start of a series.
3. **1941 – 1974** — no Faroese issues, a **33-year gap**, the longest and most notable. Again
   Danish stamps served. The 1940–41 provisionals were an emergency measure that ended when
   normal supply resumed, and were formally invalidated in **1953**.
4. **1975 – present** — I found **no evidence of any year without issues**, and given Posta's
   continuous commercial stamp programme a gap year would be surprising. But I did not check
   year-by-year, so "no gaps since 1975" is **an assumption, not a verified finding**.

The underlying reason for all three gaps is the same and is simple: for 105 of the 156 years
since 1870, the Faroes were a Danish postal district using Danish stamps. The provisionals are
punctuation marks in that continuity, not the start of separate eras.

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

### Wallpaper risk: **LOW. The Faroe Islands is NOT an agency/wallpaper issuer.**

Stating this plainly, as asked: **Faroe Islands stamps are not IGPC product, not Stamperija
product, and not agency wallpaper.** Posta runs its own philatelic programme in-house — since
1976 the Postage Stamp Department has organised all production, issuance and sale of stamps
itself. The output rate supports this: roughly **1,150 stamps over 51 years ≈ 22–23 per year**,
which is a normal, restrained European national programme. Wallpaper issuers produce that many
in a month. The subject matter is consistently Faroese (landscape, Norse and Faroese history,
birds, fish, local art, literature) rather than the tell-tale agency menu of Disney, Diana,
Elvis, dinosaurs and football.

Two mild caveats, neither amounting to wallpaper: (a) philatelic sales have historically been a
major Faroese export earner, with stamps reportedly the **second-largest source of export
revenue** at times — so the programme is unambiguously collector-facing and commercially
motivated; (b) the modern programme includes sheetlets, booklets and ATM/variable-value labels,
which inflate item counts on sites like Colnect (§1). Neither is a red flag for authenticity.

**One caution on the word "restrained":** I characterised the ~22/year rate myself by dividing
the StampWorld total by the year span. No source described Posta's policy as conservative. The
arithmetic is sound; the characterisation is mine.

### Forgery risk: **VERY HIGH — but sharply concentrated on six stamps.**

The risk profile is bimodal and this is the single most useful practical takeaway:

- **Scott/Michel 1–6 (the 1919 and 1940–41 provisionals): extreme risk.**
- **Scott/Michel 7 onward (everything from 1975): negligible risk.** Modern Faroese stamps are
  low-value, mass-produced, recent, and worth nobody's time to forge.

**Why the provisionals are so exposed.** Every one of them is a **handstamped or typeset
surcharge on a common, cheap Danish stamp**. The base stamp costs pennies and is freely
available; the entire value resides in a few lines of overprinted ink. That is the classic
highest-risk forgery structure in philately — there is no engraving to reproduce, no paper to
match, no watermark to fake. The 1919 issue is additionally very scarce: only **155 sheets** of
the 5 øre were surcharged.

**Documented forgery types** (a dedicated forgery-reference site maintains a Faroe Islands page):
- **Forged overprints** on genuine Danish base stamps — the dominant category.
- **Fake cancellations**, applied to make forged overprints look postally used and to disguise
  the surcharge under ink. Explicitly documented for the 1919 "2 øre on 5 øre".
- **Faked A/B varieties** — since Michel 2, 5 and 6 exist in two printings distinguished only by
  the **spacing between the zeros**, and the two printings carry different values, altering or
  re-faking a surcharge to convert the common version into the scarce one is an obvious attack.
  *(That this specific attack occurs is my inference from the structure of the issue; I found the
  A/B distinction documented but not an explicit statement that it is forged. Flagged in §9.)*
- **Faked covers**, since genuine 1919 and 1940–41 covers are described as "cherished rarities."

**Practical rule:** any 1919 or 1940–41 Faroese provisional offered without a recognised expert
certificate should be assumed forged until proven otherwise. This is standard advice for
overprint rarities and applies here with full force. I did **not** find a source naming the
specific expertising bodies used for Faroese material — likely candidates are the Danish and
Scandinavian expert committees, but I could not confirm which. See §9.

I found **no named forger** attached to these issues (no Sperati/Fournier-style attribution).
Absence of evidence only.

## 8. Image sourcing

### **CORRECTION: Wikimedia Commons is NOT empty for the Faroe Islands.**

The research brief states "Wikimedia Commons currently has none." **This is wrong.** Search
results confirm at least three relevant Commons entities exist:

- **`Category:Stamps of the Faroe Islands`** — exists, with files listed.
- **`Faroese stamps 1975`** — a Commons page/category specifically for the first issue.
- **`Słania's stamps for the Faroe Islands`** — a Commons gallery for the Slania-engraved
  material, which by definition includes the 1975 first series.

I could not enumerate how *many* files these hold, or their individual licence tags — Commons
categories can be thin, and their existence does not guarantee usable coverage. But the premise
that Commons has nothing does not survive checking, and this should be re-examined directly
before any sourcing plan is built on it.

### Copyright status of Faroese stamp designs

This is genuinely intricate and worth getting right:

- **Danish copyright law does not automatically extend to the Faroe Islands.** It applies only
  where brought into operation by Royal Ordinance, with modifications for local conditions. So
  "it's Danish, use the Danish rules" is **not** a safe shortcut.
- The governing instrument is the **Faroese copyright law of 2013**.
- **Term: life of the author + 70 years.**
- **Anonymous works: 70 years from first making available to the public**, if the author never
  disclosed their identity.
- **Photographic images: year of creation + 50 years** (note: this is the shorter *photographic
  picture* neighbouring right, distinct from photographic *works*).
- **There is no special provision for stamps and no special rule for government works.** Faroese
  stamps are therefore **ordinary copyrighted artistic works** under the normal life+70 term.

**Practical consequence for a 1975-onward catalogue: effectively everything is in copyright.**
Lydia Laksefoss's and Czesław Slania's Faroese work is nowhere near expiry (Slania died in 2005,
so his designs run to at least 2075). Sámal Joensen-Mikines died in **1979**, so the painting
reproduced on the 500 øre value is protected until at least the end of **2049**. There is no
public-domain tranche of Faroese stamps to harvest, because the country's stamps only begin in
1975.

The one partial exception: **Scott/Michel 1–6 are surcharged Danish stamps**, and the underlying
Danish designs (Christian X definitives, 1910s–1930s) are old enough that the underlying artwork
may well be public domain — but under **Danish** law, and the surcharge itself is arguably
below the threshold of originality. This is the only pre-1975 avenue and it needs a real
copyright review, not a guess.

**Recommended sourcing approach**, in order:
1. **Wikimedia Commons** — check the three categories above first, and use only files with
   explicit PD or free-licence tags. Do not assume a file on Commons is correctly licensed.
2. **Posta / stamps.fo** — the official issuer publishes high-quality images and a
   "Stamp catalogue 1975–2013". These are **copyrighted and not reusable without permission**,
   but Posta is a plausible party to grant a licence for a catalogue application. Worth asking.
3. **Commercial databases** (StampWorld, Colnect, Freestampcatalogue, StampData) — images are
   user-uploaded with unclear provenance and are **not** safely reusable. Useful for
   identification and cross-referencing only.
