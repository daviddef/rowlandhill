# Georgia

Base-catalogue dossier. Compiled July 2026, **revised 19 July 2026** (a July skeleton existed;
two of its claims are refuted below and marked **CORRECTED**). Every count is tagged with the
catalogue it came from; untagged counts are worthless because catalogues disagree by roughly
±20% on this country — and here they disagree about *what Georgia even is*.

**Scope:** the Caucasus republic (`GE`, Sakartvelo). **Not** the US state. **Not** South Georgia
& the South Sandwich Islands. All three collide in search and must be disambiguated at ingestion.

**1919–23 majors: 36 (Scott 2011).** ⚠️ Big Blue also records a **2014 Scott renumbering** of the
St George issue, so *Scott numbers for Georgia's first issue are not stable across editions* —
store the edition alongside the number or cross-catalogue matching will silently fail.

**1993+ majors: no reliable free figure exists.** Subtracting the classical era from the
aggregates gives a defensible band of **~700 (StampData) to ~1,075 (Colnect/Michel)**. Publish the
range, never a point estimate. Colnect's year list shows the shape: 15 (1993), 14 (1994), then
mostly 24–76 a year, 40 in 2025.

**Which to trust.** Scott for 1919–23, because it is the only figure traceable to a human reading
a physical catalogue. For the modern era, prefer Michel-derived figures over StampWorld: Michel is
the authority for the Eastern European region and StampWorld is generous with sheetlets and minor
varieties.

**Trap:** the great majority of Georgian postal *history* is not catalogued under "Georgia" at
all. Pre-1918 is Imperial **Russia**; 1924–1991 is **USSR**; part of 1922–23 is **Transcaucasia**
(or Azerbaijan, depending on catalogue — see §4). A country-keyed search over "Georgia" returns
roughly 1919–23 plus 1993–present and silently drops 130 years.

## 2. First issue

| | |
|---|---|
| **Date** | **26 May 1919** — the first anniversary of the 26 May 1918 declaration of independence |
| **Issuer** | Democratic Republic of Georgia |
| **Subject** | **St George on horseback**, spearing the dragon — patron saint and central charge of the Georgian arms |
| **Denominations** | ⚠️ **five or six — sources conflict.** Wikipedia and dcstamps say six; Big Blue's Scott-based account describes five values. Unresolved. |
| **Format** | Issued **both perforated and imperforate**, both as majors. This perf/imperf pairing is the single largest source of catalogue-number confusion for this country. |
| **Catalogue** | Opens the Scott Georgia listing. See the renumbering warning above. |
| **Printer** | **State Printing Works, Tiflis (Tbilisi)** — consistently attested; the Queen Tamar set followed from the same works |
| **Designer** | ⚠️ **"Chernavadze" — DO NOT ENCODE.** See §9. |

**Sequel issues:**
- **1920 or 1921** — **Queen Tamar (Thamar)**, r. 1184–1213, three denominations, imperforate and
  **perf 11½**. ⚠️ Wikipedia says 1921, Big Blue says 1920, StampWorld's own page titles disagree.
  **Do not assert a year.**
- **Feb 1921** — St George and Tamar remainders **overprinted** in Georgian, four lines,
  "27.1.1921 / Recognition of Independence" (the de jure Allied recognition). Applied by exiled
  officials, **probably in Italy**, and carrying **no Scott numbers**. Treat as a philatelic label
  with a real history, not a postal issue.
- **Jan–Feb 1922** — five stamps for Georgia as a Soviet republic (Georgian SSR), perf and imperf.
- **Apr 1922** — famine-relief semi-postals, **Scott B1–B4**.
- **Mar 1922 – Sep 1923** — overprinted stamps of the **Transcaucasian SFSR**.

## 3. Issuing periods

| Period | Issuing authority | What the collector actually sees |
|---|---|---|
| to 1918 | **Imperial Russia** (Russian Post) | Russian stamps used in Georgia; identifiable only by Tiflis / Batum / Kutais cancels. Catalogued under **Russia**. |
| Apr–May 1918 | Transcaucasian Democratic Federative Republic | **No stamps** in its own name — too short-lived |
| 26 May 1919 – Feb 1921 | **Democratic Republic of Georgia** | St George (1919), Queen Tamar (1920/21), Recognition overprints (Feb 1921). Under **Georgia**. |
| Feb 1921 – Mar 1922 | **Georgian SSR** (Soviet occupation from Feb 1921) | Jan–Feb 1922 five-stamp series; Apr 1922 famine-relief semi-postals. Under **Georgia** in most catalogues. |
| Mar 1922 – 30 Sep 1923 | **Transcaucasian SFSR (TSFSR / ЗСФСР)** | Star/`ЗСФСР` overprints on Russian and Armenian stamps |
| **1 Oct 1923** – 1924 | **Transcaucasian SFSR**, general issues | 1923 definitives. **Scott 1–31 under a separate "Transcaucasian Federated Republics" key** |
| 1924 – 1991 | **USSR** (Ministry of Communications) | Soviet stamps only. Under **Russia/USSR**. |
| Apr 1991 – 30 Jul 1993 | Independent Georgia, **no national stamps** | Residual Soviet stock. See §6. |
| **31 Jul 1993** – present | **Georgian Post / საქართველოს ფოსტა** (gpost.ge) | First issue: "Georgia – UN Member", 1st anniversary of UN admission, perf 13½. UPU member from 1993. |

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers (catalogue names)

```
Russian Empire ─▶ Democratic Republic of Georgia (1919–21) ─▶ Georgian SSR (1922) ─┐
                                                                                    ├─▶ Transcaucasian SFSR (1922–24)
        Armenia (1919–23) ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
        Azerbaijan (1919–23) ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                                        │
                                        ▼
                                 USSR (1924–1991) ─▶ Georgia (1993– )
```

**Predecessor chain, reading backwards:** Georgia (1993–) ← USSR/Russia (1924–91) ←
Transcaucasian Federation / TSFSR / ЗСФСР (1922–24) ← Georgia, Soviet republic issues (1922) ←
Georgia, Democratic Republic (1919–22) ← Russia, Imperial (to 1918). **Successors: none — Georgia
is a live issuer.**

🚨 **The single biggest trap is the Transcaucasian era.** Catalogues split it three ways:
*"Michel lists a set of overprints on Azerbaijani stamps for use only in Azerbaijan under
Transcaucasia. Scott and Yvert & Tellier list this issue under Azerbaijan, Stanley Gibbons lists
only the definitives."* (StampWorldHistory). **The same physical stamp lives under three different
country headings depending on catalogue.** Cross-catalogue matching will throw false negatives
unless `Transcaucasia`, `Azerbaijan` and `Georgia` are aliased for this window.

**Adjacent keys a Georgia collection bleeds into:**
- **Batum (British Occupation)** — 🚨 a **separate SG country listing**: SG 1–6 (aloe-tree, Feb
  1919, imperforate, inscribed `BATUMSKAYA POCHTA`), SG 11–18 and SG 45–53 ("BRITISH OCCUPATION"
  overprints). British forces held Batum Dec 1918 – Jul 1920. Geographically Georgian, and Georgia's
  own 2001 UPU protest claims it — but **every major catalogue files it as its own country.** Do
  not fold it into Georgia; do link it.
- **Armenia**, **Azerbaijan** — the other two Transcaucasian issuers; the same overprint-forgery
  workshops served all three.
- **Russian Post Offices in the Levant / "Russian Offices Abroad"** — investigated; **no relevance
  found.** Those cover the Ottoman Empire and China. **Not** a Georgian predecessor.

### Excluded issuers — verified, with an important qualification

- **Abkhazia** (`Republic of Abkhazia` / Аҧсны) and **South Ossetia** were declared **illegal** by
  Georgia's Ministry of Posts to the UPU in two circulars, both confirmed on PWMO's illegals index:
  - **UPU Circular 106, 29 March 1999** — Abkhazia and South Ossetia.
  - **UPU Circular 50, 29 January 2001** — extends the protest to *"the Republic of Abkhasia, the
    Republic of South Osetia, **Adjara and Batumi**."*
  Georgia asked members not to disseminate them *"in stamp catalogues or the philatelic press."*
- Most Abkhazia material is regarded as **bogus** — produced abroad purely for collectors — though
  some later issues may see genuine local use inside the disputed territory.
- ⚠️ **CORRECTED — "catalogues exclude them" is now only half true.** Reporting indicates **Michel
  added Abkhazia and began assigning issue numbers from the 2020 edition.** Scott and SG are
  believed to still exclude, but that is **not verified**. Model this as
  **issuer = illegal/unrecognised**, with a *per-catalogue listed/not-listed flag* — not a blanket
  exclusion, or Michel-sourced data will silently contradict the schema.

## 5. Inscriptions (the identification field)

| Era | Printed text | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1919 St George | `საქართველო` + **`LA GEORGIE`** | *Sakartvelo*. The French form on the first issue is **"LA GEORGIE"** — **not** "République Géorgienne" |
| 1920/21 Queen Tamar | Georgian + French for "Republic of Georgia" | ⚠️ The **exact** French string is **unverified** — sources paraphrase, none quote. `RÉPUBLIQUE GÉORGIENNE` is plausible but **must not be encoded as a searchable inscription until seen on a stamp.** |
| Feb 1921 overprint | Georgian script only, four lines, `27.1.1921` | "Recognition of Independence" |
| 1922 Georgian SSR | reported as `სსსრ` (S-S-S-R in mkhedruli); Cyrillic `ССРГ` also reported | ⚠️ Both single-sourced and unverified against an image |
| 1922–23 TSFSR overprints | **`ЗСФСР`** (Russian Cyrillic) within a star | *ZSFSR* |
| 1923 TSFSR definitives | inscribed in **Armenian, Azerbaijani, Georgian and Russian** on one stamp | Quadrilingual — a distinctive and reliable era fingerprint |
| 1924–91 | Russian only (`СССР`, `ПОЧТА СССР`) | No Georgian on Soviet definitives |
| 1993– | Latin **`GEORGIA`**, some **`GEORGIAN POST`**, plus `საქართველო` | Operator `საქართველოს ფოსტა` (*Sakartvelos Posta*) |

**Currency:** *kapeiki / rubli* (1919–23) → kuponi/coupons in the messy 1993–95 transition →
**lari / tetri** (`ლარი` / `თეთრი`) from 1995.

**Script notes for a parser.** Georgian uses **mkhedruli** (asomtavruli/nuskhuri do not appear on
stamps). Mkhedruli is **unicameral** — there is no capitalisation, so `საქართველო` has exactly one
casing. Transliteration varies wildly in dealer data: ***Sakartvelo / Saqartvelo / Sak'art'velo***
— alias all three. `Tiflis` (pre-Soviet) vs `Tbilisi` (post-1936) is the same trap for postmark
place names.

**The three-way second-script test dates a Georgian stamp almost by itself:** mkhedruli runs
throughout, with **French** as second script 1919–21, **Cyrillic** 1922–23, **Latin** 1993–present.

## 6. Gaps

| Years | Status | Why |
|---|---|---|
| to 1919 | No Georgian stamps | Russian Empire issues in use |
| **1924 – 1991 (≈67 years)** | **No Georgian issues at all** | Absorbed into the USSR. The largest gap of any European issuer, and the reason naive "Georgian stamp" counts look absurdly small for a country with this postal history. |
| **Apr 1991 – 30 Jul 1993 (≈2 years)** | Independent, **no stamps** | Documented gap, not a cataloguing artefact |
| 1921 (Feb–Dec) | Thin | Sovietisation |

⚠️ **Myth: "Georgia overprinted Soviet stamps at independence."** **False.** Georgia is
specifically noted as *not* having done so, diverging from the Baltic / Central Asian / Ukrainian
pattern; Soviet stock simply continued in use. Overprints **were** applied in **1994 — but to
Georgian stamps, not Soviet ones.** Any "Georgia 1991/92 overprint on a USSR stamp" is suspect by
default.

⚠️ **Myth: "Georgia's first stamp was 1918."** **False as to stamps.** Independence was declared
26 May 1918; the first stamps came **26 May 1919**, exactly a year later. The error is widespread
because collections are labelled "1918–1921" after the *republic's* span.

**Soft boundary:** sources say Soviet stamps ran "1924–1993"; the exact end of Transcaucasian
validity in Georgia (Sep 1923 vs into 1924) could not be pinned.

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

### 1919–23: HIGH — but the risk is concentrated, not uniform

- The Russian Civil War period is among the most forged fields in philately. Wikipedia's
  *Russian philatelic forgeries*: *"Some issues of the Civil War have also been forged, such as the
  definitives issued by Azerbaijan, Georgia and Armenia."* Forgery of an **overprint or surcharge on
  a common basic stamp** was the dominant pattern — the forger needs only a handstamp and a cheap
  genuine stamp.
- **The nuance worth encoding:** the *basic* Georgian issues are cheap (Big Blue: all nine National
  Republic stamps catalogue **under $1**; Soviet Georgia mostly **$3–$9**). Low value = low forgery
  incentive. One source states Georgia 1919–23 is *"well-studied and not very complex, **except for
  some of the handstamp surcharges**."* **Forgery pressure tracks price — and price sits in the
  overprints.**
- **Named risk points:**
  - **1922–23 Soviet Georgia surcharges/overprints** — hyperinflation-era handstamps. The canonical
    target.
  - **TSFSR `ЗСФСР` star overprints** on Russian/Armenian base stamps.
  - **Feb 1921 "Recognition of Independence" overprints** — exile product, no Scott numbers.
  - **Famine-relief semi-postals (Scott B1–B4)** — Scott lists them **perforate only**, yet
    imperforate specimens exist. **Any imperforate B1–B4 is outside catalogue and needs
    expertising.**
  - **Perf ↔ imperf conversion** — perforations added to, or trimmed from, a genuine stamp to
    manufacture the scarcer variety. A live alteration vector across the whole classical period.
- **Named forgers:** **François Fournier** (Russian-area high values; his workshop used both wholly
  forged stamps and genuine stamps as bases for fake overprints). For **Batum** — routinely mis-sold
  as Georgia — *"all the stamps of Batum under British occupation have been extensively forged"*,
  principally by **Nino Imperato of Genoa** in the 1920s, whose Type II forgeries **may outnumber
  genuine examples in collections**. Diagnostic: genuine kopeck values show **six dots** in the
  upper value tablet; white paper is a bad sign.
- **Database rule:** flag *expertisation-required* by default on any 1919–23 Georgian item whose
  value derives from an **overprint**, a **perforation variety**, or a **colour error**. Detailed
  provenance is the consensus authentication method for this area.

### 1993+: LOW forgery risk. **Georgia is NOT a wallpaper issuer — stated plainly.**

- Volumes are **modest**: 15 (1993), 14 (1994), mostly **24–76/year** thereafter, 40 in 2025
  (Colnect). A wallpaper issuer runs 200–600+ a year.
- **Georgia does not appear on illegal/bogus issuer lists** — it appears on them as the
  **complainant**, having filed the UPU circulars against Abkhazia and South Ossetia.
- **No evidence of an IGPC / Stamperija-style agency contract** was found.

**Two contamination exceptions, neither Georgia's fault:**
1. **Abkhazia and South Ossetia** — heavy bogus output, UPU-illegal, sold as "Georgia" on
   marketplace sites. This, not forgery of Georgian Post issues, is the real modern-era risk.
2. **AI-generated dealer pages.** Search results for early Georgia now include auto-generated
   "dictionary" and value-guide pages carrying confident but unsourced Scott numbers, dates and
   attributions. Any figure appearing *only* on such a page was treated as unverified here.

## 8. Image sourcing

⚠️ **CORRECTED — the previous skeleton's copyright section was wrong in both directions.** It
claimed Commons had "effectively nothing" and that 1993+ Georgian issues are "IN COPYRIGHT."
Neither survives checking.

**Copyright status is unusually favourable.** Per Commons' *Copyright rules by territory / Georgia*:

- Term is **life + 70 years**; anonymous/pseudonymous **70 years from publication**; terms run to
  year-end. Georgia joined **Berne on 16 May 1995**.
- **Georgian law explicitly excludes from copyright** *"official state symbols (flags,
  coats-of-arms, anthems, rewards, banknotes, other state symbols and insignia)"* and official
  documents. **Commons treats Georgian stamps as falling under this exemption and tags them
  `{{PD-GE-exempt}}`.** If that reading holds, **modern Georgian stamps are reusable** — rare, and
  the opposite of the usual position.
- Works whose protection had **already expired when the 1999 law took effect did not have
  protection restored**. The 1919–23 issues are clear on age regardless (>100 years).
- Soviet-era (1924–91) material requires the **separate Soviet/Russian analysis**, not the Georgian
  one — largely moot here, since Georgia issued nothing in that window. Soviet state stamp designs
  are widely treated as PD in the Russian Federation; low-risk but not guaranteed in all
  jurisdictions.
- **Scan vs design:** the *design* being PD does not settle the *photograph*. A faithful scan is
  generally held to lack originality in the US (*Bridgeman* reasoning) but the position differs
  elsewhere. Dealer and auction sites assert rights over their photos regardless of the stamp's
  status.

**What is actually on Commons** (measured via the Commons API, 19 Jul 2026):

| Category | Files (direct) |
|---|---|
| `Stamps of Georgia (country)` | **0** — a container of 9 subcategories only |
| `Stamps of the Transcaucasian Socialist Federative Soviet Republic` | **55** |
| `Stamps of Democratic Republic of Georgia` | **8** |
| `Stamps of South Ossetia` | 5 — *illegal issuer, do not ingest as Georgia* |
| `Postage stamps of Abkhazia` | 2 — *ditto* |
| `Stamps of Batumi` | 0 |
| `Stamps of Georgia in the Russian Empire` | 0 |

Files also sit under `Stamps of Georgia (country) by year` (40 subcategories), **not counted
recursively** — the true total is higher than the 63 direct files above and **must be re-measured
recursively** before planning coverage.

**Practical conclusion.** The classical era is thin but real (55 TSFSR + 8 DRG). The modern era is
the opportunity: the legal position appears permissive while the images are largely absent.
Lawful routes, in order of cleanliness: (a) own scans of stamps held — moots the photograph
question entirely; (b) Commons `PD-GE-exempt` material; (c) explicit permission from **Georgian
Post**; (d) institutional collections (Smithsonian NPM releases some material openly — verify item
by item, never collection-wide). **StampWorld and Colnect are not reusably licensed and both block
automated fetch** (403 and an Anubis proof-of-work wall respectively). **No sanctions or trade
restrictions** apply to Georgian philatelic material.

## 9. Uncertain — could not verify

- **The 1993+ major-number count in any catalogue.** Range ~700–1,075. No point estimate is honest.
- **Stanley Gibbons and Scott totals for modern Georgia**, and whether Scott continued the
  pre-Soviet sequence past 18/36 or restarted at 1 in 1993. **Not established — do not assume.**
- **Whether the 2014 Scott renumbering changed the 1919–23 total of 36** or only redistributed
  numbers within it.
- **Whether the 1919 first issue has five or six denominations.**
- **Whether Queen Tamar is 1920 or 1921.** Three sources, two answers.
- ⚠️ **The designer "Chernavadze."** Repeated across search summaries in *identical* phrasing
  ("designed by Chernavadze and printed by the State Printing Works in Tiflis") — the signature of
  a single upstream sentence copied outward. It may be an old catalogue note; it may be AI-generated
  dealer filler (cf. the fabricated "R.A. Harrison" in the Nauru dossier). It is also not a
  well-formed Georgian surname and may be a mangled transliteration. **Do not encode a designer for
  the 1919 issue.**
- **The exact French inscription on the Tamar issue.** Paraphrased everywhere, quoted nowhere.
- **The 1922 Georgian SSR inscription** (`სსსრ` and/or `ССРГ`). Single-sourced each.
- **Printing method of the 1919 St George issue.** The State Printing Works, Tiflis is confirmed;
  lithography vs typography is **not**. Lithography is the usual assumption for this material —
  an assumption, not a fact.
- **Exact end of Transcaucasian validity in Georgia** (Sep 1923 vs into 1924).
- **Whether the 1922 Georgian SSR five-stamp set keys to Georgia or to Russia** in Scott/SG.
- **Whether Scott and SG currently list Abkhazia.** Only the Michel-2020 addition is sourced.
- **Which Michel volume covers Georgia.** One source states it is *not* in *Osteuropa* (E15). This
  matters for anyone buying a catalogue to verify counts.
- **Which 1994 Georgian overprints exist and on what.** Confirmed to exist; not enumerated.
- **Recursive Commons file count.** Not measured.
- **No specialist society monograph was located.** Rossica / BSRP have almost certainly published
  on Georgian and Transcaucasian forgeries; none of it is free online. **Physical literature is the
  next step here, not more searching.**
