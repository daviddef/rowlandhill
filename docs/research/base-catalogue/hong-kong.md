# Hong Kong

Base catalogue skeleton. Research date: 2026-07-19.

**Critical structural note for the database:** Hong Kong is not one clean, continuous catalogue
run. At least three separate bodies of material that "belong" to Hong Kong's postal history sit
under **other** catalogue headings entirely — pre-1862 Great Britain use, the Japanese
occupation (its own catalogue entity, not "Hong Kong"), and — going the other direction — the
1917–1930 Treaty Port material is Hong Kong stamps but was used *in China*. A search keyed only
on "Hong Kong" will miss some of this unless the succession/alias graph accounts for it. See §3
and §4.

**Which to trust.** Treat **StampWorld (~2,875)** and **Colnect (~3,195)** as the two usable
anchors, roughly **11% apart** — consistent with the ±20% variance the brief warns is normal
between aggregator catalogues, which tend to number miniature sheets, se-tenant pairs and some
varieties as separate entries. **Neither is Scott or Stanley Gibbons**, and no major-number
count from either of those two was confirmed in this pass. Given the pattern seen in other
countries in this corpus (aggregators consistently run higher than Scott/SG major-number
counts), a true Scott/SG major-number total for Hong Kong is plausibly in a **materially lower
band** than either aggregator figure — but this is an inference, not a verified figure, and
must not be entered as data. **Do not populate a count field from an unnamed catalogue.**

Neither aggregator figure as stated separates out the three non-"Hong Kong proper" bodies of
material described in §3/§4 (Treaty Ports, Japanese occupation, pre-1862 GB use) — those are
listed under different catalogue country entities, so the ~2,875–3,195 range should be read as
**Hong Kong-proper only** (1862–present, colonial + SAR).

## 2. First issue

- **Date: 8 December 1862.** Consistently verified across Wikipedia, the Hong Kong Philatelic
  Society, and secondary histories.
- **Design:** Queen Victoria portrait (chalon-style head), **engraved by Ferdinand (Jean
  Ferdinand) Joubert**.
- **Printer: De La Rue** (London), letterpress/line-engraved on unwatermarked paper — watermark
  (Crown CA) was not introduced until the following year, 1863.
- **Denominations:** sources are **internally inconsistent on the count**. Wikipedia's own text
  and the Hong Kong Philatelic Society both list the same seven face values — **2c, 8c, 12c,
  18c, 24c, 48c, 96c** — but one summarization of the HKP Society page mislabeled this list as
  "six denominations" while quoting all seven. **Treat the value list (2/8/12/18/24/48/96 cents)
  as correct and the "six" headcount as a transcription error, not a real dispute.**
- **Catalogue numbers:** individual dealer listings surfaced **Scott 1–8** and **SG 1–7 / SG 1a**
  for pieces of this set (e.g. an SG2 example, a Scott 5 and Scott 6 5-cent/12-cent single), but
  **no single source was fetched that lists the complete first-issue Scott or SG number run
  value-by-value**. Do not treat "Scott 1–8" as confirmed for all seven denominations without
  checking a physical catalogue.
- **Compulsory local use:** local stamps became **compulsory by law in 1864** (i.e., a roughly
  two-year transition from the December 1862 introduction), per the Wikipedia summary.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers — catalogue headings

Names a catalogue would actually use, and where each sits:

1. **Great Britain** — for any pre-1862 adhesive stamp usage at Hong Kong. Hong Kong itself was
   assigned the **numbered/coded obliterator "B62"** (plus a "62B" supplementary killer) as one
   of the many British-post-offices-abroad numeral cancellation codes documented in Wikipedia's
   *Coded postal obliterators* article. **This is genuinely a Great-Britain-catalogue matter,
   not a Hong Kong one, if it applies** — but see §9: this pass could **not** confirm the
   specific claim that Great Britain adhesive stamps were sold and used at Hong Kong before
   local stamps existed in 1862. What IS confirmed is that B62 was Hong Kong's assigned code and
   was used on incoming Treaty Port mail bagged to Hong Kong, and (per general philatelic
   convention for this kind of code) very likely also on Hong Kong's own early stamps. **Do not
   assert a pre-1862 Great-Britain-used-at-Hong-Kong period as confirmed fact; flag it as
   plausible-but-unverified.**
2. **Hong Kong** — 1862–1997, the main colonial run under the queens/kings above.
3. **British Post Offices in China** (a.k.a. "Treaty Ports") — catalogued by Stanley Gibbons as
   part of its **China** catalogue material, but the stamps used are Hong Kong-issue stock
   overprinted `CHINA`; SG has also been observed using a **"Z"-prefixed number** for at least
   some British-Post-Offices-in-China items sold within its Hong Kong section (an SG "Z5" was
   found for an 1862 24c green used with a British-Post-Offices-in-China cancel). **The exact
   split between "listed in the Hong Kong catalogue" and "listed in the China catalogue" was not
   fully resolved** — treat as catalogued adjacent to Hong Kong but flagged separately, and
   confirm against a physical SG or Scott China/Hong Kong volume before building a schema
   assumption.
4. **Japanese occupation of Hong Kong** (1941–1945) — a **distinct issuer entity** in at least
   one structured database (StampData lists it as issuer id 367, "Japanese occupation of Hong
   Kong," separate from the Hong Kong issuer record). Do not fold this into the Hong Kong
   colonial timeline as if it were continuous.
5. **Hong Kong** (SAR) — 1 July 1997–present, catalogued continuously under the same "Hong Kong"
   heading pre- and post-handover (unlike, say, a full country-name change) — Stanley Gibbons'
   own 7th-edition product description explicitly states the catalogue "lists all stamps of the
   former British colony and the Special Administrative Region," i.e., one catalogue, one
   heading, spanning the handover. **This is an important confirmed fact: 1997 is not a
   catalogue break the way a full sovereignty change usually is.**

## 6. Gaps

- **1841–8 Dec 1862 — no Hong Kong adhesive stamps at all.** ~21 years with only handstamped
  prepayment markings. Not a "gap" in an ongoing series so much as the pre-stamp era; still
  relevant because collectors/searches sometimes expect "Hong Kong" material starting at 1841.
- **1891–1941 — a 50-year near-drought in commemoratives.** One source states only **three**
  further commemorative sets were issued in this window: the Silver Jubilee of George V (1935),
  the Coronation of George VI (1937), and the Centenary of British rule (1941). Definitives
  continued throughout; this is a gap in commemoratives specifically, not in issuing generally.
  One popularly repeated explanation for the gap is the chaotic 1891 Jubilee overprint sale (see
  the flagged item below) — **this causal claim (deterred future commemoratives because of the
  1891 violence) is speculative, phrased with "perhaps," and should not be stated as established
  fact.**
- **No King Edward VIII stamps.** Confirmed by Wikipedia's summary — Edward VIII's short reign
  (1936) produced no Hong Kong issue, consistent with most of the Empire.
- **1941–early 1942 — no postage stamps at all** during the immediate transition to occupation;
  postal service resumed under Japanese administration in early 1942 using standard Japanese
  definitives (0.5 sen–10 yen, "20 or 21" denominations per one source — the exact count of
  denominations was not resolved to a single number).
- **August–autumn 1945 — no stamps** immediately after Japanese surrender; mail reportedly
  carried free, with octagonal `HONG KONG 1945 POSTAGE PAID` handstamps used before pre-war
  Hong Kong stamp stock could be recovered and reissued.

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

**Wallpaper/agency risk: Hong Kong is explicitly NOT identified as a wallpaper issuer.** No
source found ties Hongkong Post to IGPC, Stamperija, or any comparable third-party stamp agency,
and no UPU illegal-issue circular naming Hong Kong was found in this pass. Hongkong Post
maintains its own stamp-issuing programme and publishes its own annual issues (e.g. the 2025
definitives, a 16-stamp landmarks set) directly, which is the pattern of a legitimate postal
administration rather than an agency client. **This is a negative finding from a limited
search, not an audited clearance** — treat as "no evidence found," not as "proven absent."

**Forgery risk is real and concentrated in the classic (Queen Victoria) era, and is explicitly
flagged by name in dedicated forgery references** (stampforgeries.com maintains pages
specifically titled "Stamp forgeries of Hong Kong" and "...Queen Victoria surcharged"):

- **Surcharge forgeries.** The genuine 1876–77 surcharges (`16/cents.`, `28/cents.`), the 1880
  5c/10c surcharges, and the 1879 `3/CENTS`, `5/CENTS`, `THREE` surcharges on postal cards are
  all named as forgery targets.
- **Named forgers active on Hong Kong material:** Spiro Brothers, Fournier, Oneglia, and
  Panelli — all major 19th-century forgery workshops — are documented as having produced
  extensive counterfeit Hong Kong material from the 1862–1880 period. Fournier forgeries are
  typically **lithographed** rather than the genuine **typographed** printing, with rougher
  shading-line breaks as the tell.
- **The Treaty Port `CHINA` overprints and their cancels are a classic forgery target class** —
  consistent with the general pattern (flagged explicitly in the brief) that overprints on an
  otherwise-common base stamp are the easiest and most profitable thing to fake. This pass did
  not find a dedicated source quantifying the scale of `CHINA`-overprint forgery specifically,
  but it follows the same structural risk as other Treaty Port and provisional overprints
  worldwide, and should be treated as **high risk** by category even without a named source
  confirming Hong Kong specifically.
- **1891 Jubilee overprint — verify before dramatizing.** Multiple secondary sources (an
  industrial-history blog, a local-interest site, and Wikipedia-derived summaries) independently
  repeat a specific and dramatic claim: that the 22 January 1891 first-day sale of the Jubilee
  overprint (a 2c carmine of the 1883 issue overprinted `1841 HONG KONG JUBILEE 1891`, limited
  to 50,000 prints, 3-day sale) caused a violent crowd crush resulting in **two Portuguese
  buyers crushed to death and one Dutch sailor stabbed to death**. The specificity and
  consistency of this claim across sources leans toward it being a genuine, if colorful,
  historical incident rather than a single-source fabrication — but **no primary source
  (contemporary Hong Kong newspaper account, colonial records) was located and checked in this
  pass**, and the brief specifically warns that AI-generated dealer/history pages are now
  polluting search results with confidently-repeated claims. **Flag this as "widely repeated,
  not independently primary-sourced" rather than confirmed fact** before it goes in any
  historical-notes field.

## 8. Image sourcing

- **Wikimedia Commons currently has very little.** `Category:Stamps_of_Hong_Kong` was found to
  contain **74 files directly**, spanning roughly the 1840s (postal history/postmark items, not
  necessarily stamps) through 2004, with 10 subcategories (by year, definitives, first-day
  covers, last-day covers, overprints, postcards, revenue stamps, telegraph stamps). This is a
  **small fraction** of the ~2,875–3,195 major numbers in §1 — well under 5% coverage even
  before accounting for the fact that many of the 74 files are postal-history items (covers,
  postmarks) rather than stamp-only images. **Do not assume Commons can seed a meaningful
  Hong Kong image corpus without additional licensed sourcing.**
- **Copyright status: Hong Kong's own stamp output is very much still in copyright, right up to
  the present day, and this is explicit in law, not inferred.** The Copyright Ordinance (Cap.
  528), Hong Kong's own post-handover copyright statute (effective 27 June 1997), carries a
  **Government copyright** regime distinct from the ordinary life-plus-50-years rule (Cap. 528
  s.17(8) explicitly carves Government copyright out of the general rules, redirecting to
  ss.182–184). Per one search-result synthesis of those sections: **Government copyright
  subsists for 125 years from the end of the calendar year the work was made, OR — if
  commercially published within 75 years of creation — for 50 years from the end of the
  calendar year of that first publication.** Under either branch, a Hong Kong stamp issued in
  2026 will not enter the public domain until at least **2076** (50-year-from-publication
  branch) and potentially not until **2151** (125-year-from-creation branch) if never
  "commercially published" in the qualifying sense. **This has NOT been checked against the
  primary statute text (ss.182–184) directly — it is a search-result synthesis of secondary
  commentary, not a lawyer's reading of Cap. 528 itself, and should be verified against
  https://www.elegislation.gov.hk/hk/cap528 before being treated as legal advice.**
- **Practical implication:** essentially the entire modern Hong Kong stamp corpus (SAR-era, and
  probably most of the colonial 20th-century material too, since colonial Hong Kong government
  copyright would likely track a comparable regime) is **not freely reusable**. Pre-1900s
  material (the 1862 Victoria definitives etc.) is old enough that copyright has almost
  certainly expired under any plausible reading of any of these terms, but this was not
  separately confirmed stamp-by-stamp.
- **Hongkong Post itself is stated (via one secondary source) to own the IP in the stamps it
  releases** — consistent with, not contradicting, the Government-copyright analysis above.

## 9. Uncertain — explicitly NOT verified

1. **Any Scott or Stanley Gibbons highest/terminal catalogue number for Hong Kong.** Not found
   despite multiple targeted searches of SG's own product pages and Scott checklist vendors.
   The §1 counts are StampWorld and Colnect only.
2. **Whether Great Britain adhesive stamps were actually sold and used at Hong Kong before 8 Dec
   1862**, and if so during what exact date range, under the `B62` obliterator. Confirmed: `B62`
   is a real, documented code assigned to Hong Kong in the British-post-offices-abroad numeral
   obliterator scheme (Wikipedia's *Coded postal obliterators*). **Not confirmed:** that this
   scheme's use at Hong Kong predates 1862 rather than starting with Hong Kong's own 1862
   stamps and continuing onto Treaty Port mail bagged there. Other sources state 1841–1862
   Hong Kong prepayment relied on handstamps, with only *military* mail using GB stamps under a
   different marking ("MILITARY POST OFFICE HONG KONG"). This is a genuine open question, not a
   settled fact — treat the "pre-1862 GB-used-at-Hong-Kong" framing as a hypothesis to verify
   against a specialist British-Used-Abroad reference (e.g. a Hong Kong Study Circle paper),
   not as confirmed.

   **Follow-up check (19 Jul 2026) — the evidence now leans AGAINST the pre-1862 GB-use
   framing.** The Hong Kong Public Libraries' *Hong Kong Memory* project (a government
   institutional source, therefore better than the dealer pages) states plainly that between
   1841 and 1862 **no stamps were issued and postmarks were used to certify payment of the
   postage instead** — i.e. prepayment in cash, handstamped, not GB adhesives. Independently,
   Wikipedia's *British post offices in China* explains B62 in a treaty-port context: letters
   were bagged at the China ports and carried to Hong Kong, **where they were cancelled B62**,
   each port only receiving its own devices in the 1860s–70s. That is a mechanism for B62
   appearing on GB stamps *posted in China and processed at Hong Kong* — which is not the same
   claim as GB stamps being sold over the counter at Hong Kong before 1862.

   **Practical consequence for the database:** do **not** create a "Great Britain used in Hong
   Kong, 1841–1862" issuing period as if it were an established era. If a predecessor edge is
   wanted, the defensible one is Great Britain → Hong Kong on *administrative* grounds (the
   colony's post offices were run by the UK Postmaster General until 1860), not on the grounds
   that GB adhesives were the ordinary prepayment method there. Still needs a specialist
   reference to close.
3. **Complete first-issue (1862) Scott/SG number list, value by value.** Individual dealer
   listings gave scattered numbers (Scott 5, 6, ~1–8; SG 1a, SG 2) but no single consolidated
   confirmed list for all seven denominations.
4. **Exact wording and script of Japanese-occupation overprints.** "Office of the Governor of
   Hong Kong" / 香港總督部 was reported by a secondary source; not checked against a primary
   image.
5. **Specific Chinese-script value/denomination characters** used across different Hong Kong
   eras (e.g. any 先/仙/角 forms). An automated page-summary offered specific characters; **not
   independently verified** and explicitly flagged as unreliable in §5.
6. **The 1891 Jubilee stampede/deaths claim** — widely repeated across secondary sources but no
   primary (contemporary press/colonial record) source was checked. See §7.
7. **Precise spelling-convention rule (`HONGKONG` vs `HONG KONG`) era-by-era** across the George
   V/George VI/Elizabeth II periods. Only the 1862–1901 (`HONGKONG`) and 1903–10 (`HONG KONG`)
   and post-1997 (`HONG KONG, CHINA`) conventions are solidly confirmed.
8. **Whether the SG "Z"-prefix numbering** (e.g. SGZ5) applies broadly across the Treaty Port
   `CHINA` overprints or was specific to the single example found. Not resolved.
9. **Michel's terminal/highest number** for Hong Kong. Only scattered individual numbers found
   (e.g. Michel 2 for part of the 1862 issue).
10. **Wikimedia Commons Hong Kong file count precision** — 74 files was read from one fetched
    category summary; subcategory totals (81 "yearly," 54 FDC, 137 revenue, 167 postcards) look
    inconsistent with a 74-file parent category and were not reconciled. Treat the 74 figure and
    the subcategory figures as **not cross-checked against each other**.
11. **The Cap. 528 ss.182–184 government-copyright term** in §8 — read from secondary commentary
    synthesis, not the primary statute text. Verify directly at elegislation.gov.hk before
    relying on it for a licensing decision.
