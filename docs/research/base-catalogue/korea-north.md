# Korea (North)

**DPRK / Democratic People's Republic of Korea · 조선민주주의인민공화국**
**Date:** 19 July 2026 · **Purpose:** base-catalogue dossier. Scope, numbering, inscriptions,
and the legal question of whether we may hold the images at all.

Michel is at ~59 in 1952 where Scott is at ~189 by 1953. Scott appears to grant far more major
numbers per year in the early period (values, perf and paper varieties, reprints). Later Scott
data points — **Scott 4145** (2001, "US atrocities"), **Scott 4759** (2008, USS *Pueblo*) — put
Scott in the 4,000s by 2008, i.e. broadly in line with the KSC's own numbering, which was at
**#4325A–D** for the Gagarin overprints. ⚠️ Note the 2001/2008 numbers are cited in a
*North Korean Review* article that does not name its catalogue; they could be KSC numbers. **Do
not treat 4145/4759 as confirmed Scott numbers.**

### ⚠️ The premise that Scott "declines to list large tranches of DPRK material" does not survive checking

This is the single most important correction in this dossier. Evidence:

- Scott **actively maintains and revises** DPRK listings. Scott made ~50 value changes to early
  DPRK issues for the **2025** catalogue (Scott 1, the 20-chon Rose of Sharon, moved from $300 to
  $2,500 used), and "updated many issues from the DPRK in the 2020 catalog."
- Scott's editors **cooperate with the Korea Stamp Society**; KSS advice caused Scott to remove
  the MNH price for 1953 Scott 189A in the 2021 edition.
- The real drivers of divergence are (a) **no reliable philatelic information ever comes out of
  the DPRK**, so catalogues make different judgement calls on what was actually issued; (b) a
  large body of **"unissued" / irregular / withdrawn / re-appeared** material that catalogues
  treat inconsistently — the KSC's own 2010 catalogue lists items absent from its 2007 edition;
  (c) **US trade restrictions** thinned dealer stocks, so US-market catalogues had less material
  to examine and price. A Scott editor writing in *Linn's* noted DPRK stamps are "not prohibited
  outright by a Treasury Department embargo in the way that Cuba and Iran stamps are," but that
  US new-issue dealers do not stock them.

**Honest position: Scott lists DPRK material broadly, not selectively. The catalogues disagree on
*which items exist*, not on *whether to acknowledge the country*.**

⚠️ **Unverified and important:** the current highest Scott, Michel and SG numbers. No free source
publishes them; Scott/StampWorld/Colnect all block automated fetch. **Confirm against physical
catalogues before any of this is treated as authoritative.**

## 2. First issue

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Date | **12 March 1946** (majority of sources) — ⚠️ **16 March 1946** per Korea Stamp Society |
| Issue | Two designs: **Rose of Sharon / Mugunghwa (무궁화)** and **Samson Rock** |
| Catalogue no. | **Scott 1** = 20-chon red, Rose of Sharon (hibiscus) |
| Designer | Paek Tae Jin (백태진) — single-source, treat as unconfirmed |
| Printing | "crudely lithographed" (Wikipedia); printer **not established** |
| Authority | Soviet-occupation civil administration; the Korea Stamp Corporation dates its own founding to **12 March 1946**, the same day |

⚠️ **Date conflict, unresolved.** Wikipedia, StampWorld and multiple dealers say **12 March
1946**; the Korea Stamp Society's Korean-War postal-history article says Japanese stamps remained
valid "until the introduction of North Korean postage stamps on **16 March 1946**." The KSS is
the better-qualified source; the 12 March date is better attested and matches the KSC's founding
date. **Both dates should be carried until a physical Michel/Scott entry settles it.**

⚠️ Note the ideological oddity worth *not* over-reading: the Mugunghwa is today the **South**
Korean national flower. In 1946 it was a pan-Korean symbol. Its appearance on DPRK #1 is not an
error and not a forgery indicator.

⚠️ **The 1910–1945 trap.** There is no "Korea 1910–1945" to catalogue. A user searching "Korea"
for that period must be routed to **Japan**. Any item offered as a "Korean stamp, 1920s" is
either a Japanese stamp with a Korean cancel (legitimate, and a real collecting area) or wrong.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers

```
Korea (Joseon 1884–1897) ──▶ Korea (Korean Empire 1897–1905) ──▶ Japan (1910–1945)
                                                                      │
                                              ┌───────────────────────┴───────────────────────┐
                                              ▼                                               ▼
                            Korea (North) / DPRK  1946–                        Korea (South) / ROK  1946–
                            (Soviet zone → DPRK 1948)                          (US zone → ROK 1948)
```

**How the split works in the catalogues — the single most valuable field here:**

- **Scott** carries the 1884–1905 issues under **"Korea"**, and that numbering **continues into
  South Korea**. The 1884 10-mun is **Scott 2**; the unissued 50-mun is **Scott 4**. South Korea
  is Scott's *continuation* of "Korea"; **North Korea starts a brand-new sequence at Scott 1
  (1946)**. So *Scott 1 is ambiguous across two countries* — Korea/South Korea Scott 1 is the
  1884 5-mun, and Korea (North) Scott 1 is the 1946 20-chon Rose of Sharon. **Any ingestion
  keyed on "Korea Scott 1" without a country discriminator will collide.**
- **Stanley Gibbons** groups all of it in one volume, **Part 18 — *Japan and Korea***
  (5th ed., 2008, 704 pp.), covering "Japan, Korean Empire, North Korea, South Korea and
  Ryukyus" as separate listings within one book. ⚠️ Whether SG continues Korean Empire numbering
  into South Korea, as Scott does, is **not established** — do not assume symmetry with Scott.
- **The KPA / Korea Stamp Corporation catalogue does something neither Western catalogue does:**
  its 10th edition (2016) **includes the 1884–1903 issues** as the "period of Feudal Joson
  Dynasty," i.e. **the DPRK claims the Joseon issues as its own predecessor lineage.** This means
  the *same physical stamp* can carry a "Korea" Scott number, a "South Korea"-lineage SG number,
  and a DPRK KSC number. This is a genuine, load-bearing edge for the succession graph.

**Catalogue names to alias:** Korea (North) · North Korea · DPRK · Democratic People's Republic
of Korea · Nordkorea (Michel) · Corée du Nord · 조선 / 조선민주주의인민공화국.

## 5. Inscriptions — the identification field

| Period | Printed text | Transliteration / meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1884–1905 (predecessor) | `COREA` / `CORÉE` in Latin; 大朝鮮 in hanja | "Corea", "Great Joseon" | ⚠️ spelled **COREA**, not "Korea" — searching only "Korea" misses the first issue |
| **1946–1947 (DPRK)** | **죠선우표**, printed **right-to-left** (so it reads `표우선죠` in modern left-to-right transcription) | *Jyoseon upyo* — "Korean postage" | note the archaic **죠** (*jyo*), not 조 |
| **1948–present** | **조선우표**, left-to-right | *Joseon upyo* — "Korean postage" | the **죠 → 조** change is the single cleanest 1947/48 dividing line |
| Full state name | **조선민주주의인민공화국** | *Joseon Minjujuui Inmin Konghwaguk* — "Democratic People's Republic of Korea" | |
| Generic | **우표** | *upyo* — "postage stamp" | |
| **Late 1977 –** | **`DPR KOREA`** (also seen `DPR of KOREA`) in Latin script | — | export-market issues; per KSS, "North Korean stamps in late 1977 started to have in English 'DPR Korea'" |
| Currency | **원** (*won*) and **전** (*chon* / *jeon*) | 100 chon = 1 won | |

**Script / hanja.** The DPRK **abolished hanja (Chinese characters) in June 1949** on Kim Il
Sung's order, with the transition already well advanced through 1946–47. **Early DPRK issues may
therefore carry hanja; post-1949 issues should be hangul-only.** Presence of hanja on a stamp
inscribed 조선우표 is a strong pre-1950 indicator.

**Currency reforms.**
- **6 Dec 1947** — Central Bank of the DPRK established; first North Korean won replaces the
  Korean yen. Denominations in 원/전.
- **1959** — revaluation at **100 old won : 1 new won**, to unwind Korean-War inflation. Face
  values on stamps reset sharply downward across this boundary. ⚠️ The task brief names a "1957
  currency reform" — **I found no 1957 reform.** The 1957 date in DPRK philately belongs to the
  **1955–57 reprint programme**, not to currency. Treat "1957 currency reform" as an error.
- Post-2002, **전 (chon) denominations stopped being used** on stamps — a KSS-cited forgery test:
  a modern "DPRK" stamp denominated in chon, especially with the chon character rotated
  sideways, is a fake.

**Negative identifier (very useful):** the **taegeuk / monad (yin-yang) symbol never appears on a
North Korean stamp.** It appears on most South Korean stamps after late 1953. Conversely, early
South Korean stamps (1946 – Jun 1948) *also* read 조선우표 — the discriminator there is that South
Korean values are given in **Latin letters**, and from July 1948 South Korea switched to
**대한민국** (*Daehan Minguk*, "Republic of Korea").

## 6. Gaps

⚠️ **I could not verify a single clean year of zero issues.** What is established:

- **Korean War 1950–53** disrupted issuing and destroyed stock. Michael Rogers: many 1946–52
  stamps "were destroyed during the Korean War," and "comprehensive collections are few and far
  between." The Korea Stamp Society's two-part study of DPRK wartime postal operations is
  **members-only**, so the year-by-year issuing record is behind a paywall.
- Issues *do* exist dated within the war (e.g. the 1952 "7 Years Liberation" set, Mi 59; a 1953
  500-won, Scott 189A), so **1950–53 is a period of scarcity and disruption, not a total gap.**
- No evidence of any other multi-year gap. Post-1974 issuing is continuous and heavy.

**Do not encode a gap for North Korea without a year-level source.**

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

### Is it a wallpaper issuer? **Yes — but by a different mechanism than the usual agency mills.**

Evidence, not folklore:

1. **The export motive is documented and admitted.** From 1974 the DPRK began issuing
   "wide-ranging themes (such as Joan of Arc, airships, sports and wildlife)" explicitly to earn
   foreign currency (Wikipedia). By the 1980s, "North Korea recognised that stamp collecting
   could generate foreign currency ... at a time when many other exports were restricted by
   sanctions," and "began mass-producing stamps specifically for collectors abroad. **Most of
   these were issued as CTO stamps**" (Koryo Tours).
2. **Some issues genuinely never reached the domestic market.** The *North Korean Review* article
   states stamps depicting the British royal family were "probably impossible to buy in North
   Korea" and "went **straight from the printers in Paris and other cities to stamp shops of the
   Western world**." A KSS forum contributor reports KSC #454 "was not sold in the USSR" and is
   rare unused but common CTO or on cover. **This claim checks out for specific issues; it is
   NOT true of the programme as a whole** — DPRK stamps are genuinely used domestically, and the
   propaganda issues in particular were "for internal indoctrination."
3. **The DPRK's issuing is not agency-outsourced.** Unlike Stamperija/IGPC clients, the issuer is
   a state body — the **Korea Stamp Corporation (조선우표사)**, HQ Pyongyang, offices in Beijing
   and Moscow, which both issues the stamps *and* publishes the catalogue. Distribution to the
   West runs through dealers (e.g. William van der Bijl, Utrecht) rather than a design agency.
4. **Official reprints for export.** In **1955–57** the KSC produced reprints of most 1955–56
   issues "created for and sold to overseas collectors in exchange for hard currency." These are
   finer-printed, on whiter wove paper, and sometimes differ in size from the originals. They are
   **genuine government product, not forgeries** — but they are not the original stamps, and
   catalogues price them separately.
5. **North Korea is *not* on the UPU illegal-stamp circulars.** The KSC is the legitimate UPU
   issuer. The criticism is cynicism, not illegitimacy. (⚠️ Also note the PWMO's view that the
   UPU circulars and WNS are unreliable in both directions — absence from the list is weak
   evidence.)

**Verdict: genuine but philatelically cynical.** DPRK stamps are real, legally issued stamps from
a UPU member. A very large share of post-1980 material is CTO produced for export, and a minority
of issues appear never to have been sold domestically. This is a *provenance and value* problem,
not an *authenticity* problem. CTO material holds the lowest value; genuinely postally used
examples are rare and command the most interest.

### Classic forgery risk — **real but narrow.**

⚠️ Contrary to the usual assumption, **wholesale forgery is not the dominant risk for DPRK.** The
documented cases are specific:

- **Recent "peace talk" stamps and souvenir sheets (2018–19) are counterfeits**, per the Korea
  Stamp Society. Tells: Western d/m/y date order instead of Korean y/m/d; **chon (전)
  denominations, rotated sideways, after chon stopped being used post-2002**; missing issue year;
  and a sheet showing both Korean flags, which contradicts DPRK unification doctrine. Produced in
  **Ukraine/Russia**, sold via Chinese and Russian eBay/Delcampe sellers.
- **Modern DPRK forgeries are hard to detect when unused** — "the techniques used in making the
  stamps are identical."
- **1955–57 reprints** are routinely sold as originals; distinguishing them "is difficult even
  for philatelic experts."
- ⚠️ **"Office 39" counterfeiting.** Reported via 1977 defector testimony as a Pyongyang facility
  engaged in counterfeit operations including stamps. **I could not corroborate the stamp element
  from a primary source.** Office 39's documented activity is currency and cigarettes. **Flag as
  unverified folklore until a citable source is found.**
- The **Korean Empire (1884–1905) predecessor material is heavily forged** — the KSS publishes a
  members-only *Handbook of Philatelic Forgeries — Korean Empire*, with articles on the 1884
  10-mun and the unissued 50-mun. **The forgery risk sits upstream in the "Korea" lineage, not in
  the DPRK lineage.**

### Propaganda / anti-US material

Legitimate KSC issues, catalogued normally. Examples: 2001 anti-US-atrocity issue (#4145),
issued per its own press release for "education in class consciousness"; 2008 USS *Pueblo*
issue (#4759). These are among the issues that genuinely *were* for domestic use. Collector
status: normal catalogued stamps, with a strong specialist/academic following (one scholar
proposes "STAMPINT" as an intelligence-analysis category). They are **not** illegal issues and
**not** excluded from catalogues.

## 8. Image sourcing and legality

### Copyright

- DPRK **acceded to the Berne Convention on 28 April 2003**.
- Governed by the **Copyright Act of 2001** (amended since). Term: **50 years p.m.a.**; for works
  authored by an institution, enterprise or organisation — which is what a KSC stamp is —
  **50 years from publication**.
- The pre-2006 exemption for "documents for state management, current news or information data"
  was narrowed in 2006, and the qualifier "unless commercial purpose is pursued" added.
- **Wikimedia Commons treats North Korean stamps as copyrighted with no stamp exemption.**
  `{{PD-North Korea}}` applies only where the corporate work was **published before 1 January
  1976**. Commons has run deletion requests against DPRK stamp uploads.
- **Practical effect: DPRK stamps published before 1976 are arguably PD; everything from 1976
  onward — i.e. the entire export era — is not.**

### Available reusable images: essentially none

`Category:Stamps of North Korea` on Commons holds **1 file**; `Postage stamps of North Korea`
holds **3**. That is the entire freely-licensed corpus. Compare ~7,500 catalogued stamps.
**Coverage available to us from Commons: <0.1%.**

### ⚠️ Sanctions — a real constraint, not a theoretical one

This is a genuine legal question for the project and the answer is **jurisdiction-dependent and
not settled by anything I could read for free.**

**United States.** Executive Order **13570** (2011), implemented in the North Korea Sanctions
Regulations, **31 CFR Part 510**, prohibits the importation into the US of goods, services and
technology from North Korea, directly or indirectly, without an OFAC licence.

- The **Berman Amendment (1988)**, as expanded by the **Free Trade in Ideas Act (1994)**, removes
  OFAC's authority over trade in "**information or informational materials**," and
  **31 CFR § 510.312** defines these as "publications, films, posters, phonograph records,
  photographs, microfilms, microfiche, tapes, compact disks, CD ROMs, **artworks**, and news wire
  feeds."
- ⚠️ **But § 510.312(a)(2) restricts "artworks" to HTS headings 9701, 9702 and 9703** — paintings,
  engravings/prints, and sculpture. **Postage stamps are HTS heading 9704**, which is *not* in the
  list. **On a plain reading, collectible postage stamps do not qualify as "artworks" under the
  informational-materials exemption.** Whether they qualify as "publications" is arguable and, so
  far as I can find, **untested**.
- A Scott editor writing in *Linn's* said DPRK stamps are "not prohibited outright by a Treasury
  Department embargo in the way that Cuba and Iran stamps are," but that US dealers do not stock
  them. That is a philatelic journalist's characterisation, **not legal advice, and it appears in
  tension with EO 13570.** Note US dealers such as Raritan list DPRK **only up to 1990**.
- **Note also § 510.517**, covering transactions related to patents, trademarks, **copyrights and
  other intellectual property** — directly relevant if we ever tried to *license* DPRK stamp
  images from the KSC.

**European Union.** Council Regulation **(EU) 2017/1509**, in force since 1 September 2017, bans
imports of specified categories (coal, iron, minerals, textiles, petroleum, luxury goods per
Annex VIII). ⚠️ **It is a sectoral list, not a blanket import ban.** Whether stamps fall under
"luxury goods" in Annex VIII is **not established** — Annex VIII must be checked line by line.

**United Kingdom.** *The Democratic People's Republic of Korea (Sanctions) (EU Exit) Regulations
2019* (SI 2019/411), in force 1 Jan 2021. Per DBT **Notice to Importers 2945**, there are **four**
import prohibitions — arms/materiel, dual-use, other WMD-related goods, and anything in Chapter
93 of the Goods Classification table. **Stamps are Chapter 97, not 93.** On this reading, **UK
import of DPRK stamps is not prohibited** — the most permissive of the four jurisdictions.

**Australia.** Broadest of all: per DFAT, **"Australian law prohibits the import from the DPRK of
any item that is not an 'import sanctioned good', food or medicine, if the import is not
authorised by a sanctions permit."** ⚠️ Read literally this is a **near-total import ban**, and
DPRK stamps would require a permit.

**UN Security Council.** UNSCR sanctions (1718 and successors) are sectoral — arms, minerals,
coal, textiles, seafood, luxury goods. **No UNSCR I found names philatelic material.**

### Bottom line for Rowland

| Jurisdiction | Import of DPRK stamps | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| US | ⚠️ Likely prohibited without licence under EO 13570 / 31 CFR 510; the informational-materials carve-out probably does **not** reach HTS 9704 | low — needs counsel |
| EU | Probably permitted (sectoral bans only) unless Annex VIII luxury goods captures them | low |
| UK | Probably permitted (four narrow prohibitions, none Chapter 97) | medium |
| Australia | ⚠️ Likely requires a sanctions permit | low–medium |

**Recommendation:** treat DPRK **image acquisition and physical acquisition as separate
questions**. We are not importing goods; we are reproducing designs. The binding constraint on
*images* is **copyright** (post-1976 KSC works are protected for 50 years from publication, and
Commons will not host them), not sanctions. But if the project ever buys physical DPRK material,
or pays the KSC for a licence, **that is a sanctions transaction and needs counsel** — the same
counsel already queued for the Scott numbering question in `CLAUDE.md`.

## 9. Uncertain — flagged, not asserted

- **Current highest Scott, Michel and SG numbers for Korea (North).** Not established. Scott,
  StampWorld and Colnect all block automated fetch.
- **Whether Scott 4145 / 4759 are Scott or KSC numbers.** The source article does not say.
- **First-issue date: 12 vs 16 March 1946.** Genuinely conflicting sources.
- **Printer of the 1946 first issue.** Not established. "Crudely lithographed" is the only
  description found; no printing house named.
- **Designer "Paek Tae Jin".** Single source. ⚠️ Given the AI-generated-dealer-page problem noted
  in the Nauru dossier, treat unconfirmed attributions like this as suspect by default.
- **Colnect's 8,881 and StampWorld's 7,571.** Both from search summaries, neither confirmed by
  direct fetch. StampWorld's page title reads "1946–2026"; Colnect's index paginates to 889.
- **Year-by-year issuing during 1950–53.** KSS's authoritative study is members-only.
- **Whether SG continues Korean Empire numbering into South Korea** (as Scott does) or restarts.
- **Whether the SG *Japan & Korea* Part 18 has an edition after the 5th (2008).**
- **Total in the KPA/KSC catalogue as *majors*.** "Over 7,200 designs" counts designs and the
  catalogue also lists non-stamp items; the two are not comparable.
- **"Office 39 counterfeited stamps."** Uncorroborated.
- **Whether stamps are "luxury goods" under EU Reg. 2017/1509 Annex VIII.** Not checked
  line-by-line.
- **Whether postage stamps count as "publications" under 31 CFR 510.312.** Appears untested.
- **The claimed "1957 currency reform."** ⚠️ I found no such reform. The reforms are **1947** and
  **1959**. 1957 is the reprint programme.
