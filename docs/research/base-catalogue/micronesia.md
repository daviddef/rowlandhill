## 1. Headline counts

FSM sits in the **US postal orbit** — the USPS carries FSM mail, FSM is inside the
US ZIP code system, and US domestic postal rates apply. Consequently **Scott is
the primary cataloguing authority** and lists the country in full in Volume 4.

| Catalogue | Approx. major numbers | Confidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scott** (primary) | **~1,000–1,400 major numbers**, 1984–present | **Low–Medium — ESTIMATE, not verified** | Scott 1–66 + C1–C38 covers 1984–1988 only (see caveat). No verified current high number found. |
| **Michel** | Substantially **higher** than Scott | Medium | Michel numbers each stamp in a sheetlet individually. |
| **StampWorld** | Listed 1984–2017 at time of research | Medium | Site blocks automated fetch (HTTP 403); count not directly read. |
| **Colnect** | **~2,496 commemoratives** (plus definitives/other formats) | Low–Medium | From search snippet only; Colnect page is behind bot-detection. Counts individual stamps, so far exceeds Scott majors. |
| **Stanley Gibbons** | Uncertain | — | No SG figure located. SG does list FSM but no count verified. |

### Where the catalogues diverge — a concrete, documented example

This is the single most useful data point found, and it is verifiable on Colnect:

> FSM **Millennium** issue — catalogued as **Michel 947–963** (17 separate
> numbers) but as **Scott 377** (ONE number), Yvert 813–829.

**Seventeen Michel numbers = one Scott number.** FSM's later output is dominated
by large multi-design sheetlets, and **Scott collapses an entire sheetlet into a
single major number with lettered minors**, whereas Michel/Yvert/Colnect number
each design. Any count you quote is therefore **meaningless without naming the
catalogue**. Expect Michel/Colnect totals to run **roughly 2–4x the Scott major
count** for FSM specifically.

⚠️ **CAVEAT on the 1984–1988 figure.** The "Scott 1–66, C1–C38" range for
1984–1988 came from a search-engine summary, not from Scott itself. It is
plausible and internally consistent, but **treat as unconfirmed.**

⚠️ **MYTH FLAG.** A search result during this research asserted that FSM
"has issued stamps through at least Scott numbers in the 1100+ range,
referencing a Millennium series with Scott numbers 1100–1150." **This is
fabricated.** "1000–2000" and "1100–1150" are the **historical year ranges
printed in the Millennium sheetlet designs** (i.e. "12th Century 1100–1150"),
not catalogue numbers. This is a textbook example of an AI-generated or
scraped dealer listing manufacturing a false catalogue attribution. **Do not
propagate it.** A separate snippet claiming "approximately 2,922 stamps
1984–2017" is likewise unverified.

| Scott numbers | Believed **Scott 1–4** | Medium — consistent with sources, not directly confirmed against Scott |
| Printer | **Uncertain** | — |

### ⚠️ The "10 July 1984" date is NOT supported

The task brief flagged 10 July 1984 as "commonly cited" and asked for a check.
**It did not survive.** Two independent sources — **StampData**, which lists the
1984 issues with per-issue dates, and a corroborating search of first-day-cover
material — both give **12 July 1984**. Wikipedia says only "1984". **No source
found in this research gives 10 July.**

Recommendation: **use 12 July 1984**, flagged Medium–High. Confirm against a
physical Scott Volume 4 before treating as settled. The two dates are close
enough that a transposition or a differing "first day of sale vs. official
inauguration" distinction is plausible.

**Printer: genuinely unknown.** House of Questa (a British security printer)
is documented as printing **Palau's** 1983 first issue and **FSM stamps in
1990** — but **no source confirms Questa printed the 1984 FSM first issue.**
Do not assume it. Marked Uncertain.

### Other 1984 issues (StampData)
- 12 July 1984 — main definitive issue, 16 stamps (explorers and landmarks)
- 21 September 1984 — Ausipex '84
- 20 December 1984 — Christmas 1984

## 3. Issuing periods

| Era | Dates | Issuing authority | Stamps used |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Spanish East Indies / Spanish Caroline Islands** | to 1899 | Spain | **NONE issued.** Wikipedia: *"The Spanish authorities issued no postage stamps or postmarks for the islands."* |
| **German colonial** | 1 June 1899 – Oct 1914 | German Empire (Deutsche Reichspost) | **CAROLINE ISLANDS / KAROLINEN** — separate catalogue country |
| **Japanese occupation → South Seas Mandate (Nan'yō)** | Oct 1914 – 1944/45 | Empire of Japan | **Stamps of JAPAN.** No separate mandate issues. |
| **US Navy / US Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands (TTPI)** | 1944/47 – 1984 | United States | **Stamps of the UNITED STATES.** No separate TTPI catalogue country. |
| **FSM Postal Services** | 12 July 1984 – present | FSM Postal Services | **FSM** own issues |

**Notes.**
- Germany acquired the Carolines from Spain **1 June 1899**; first overprints
  **12 October 1899**. (High confidence.)
- The TTPI start date varies by source — **1944** (military occupation),
  **1946**, or **1947** (formal UN trusteeship). Sources genuinely disagree;
  Wikipedia's FSM and Marshalls articles say 1946 and 1947 respectively.
- FSM's Compact of Free Association with the US: **effective 1986**. Note the
  postal service (1984) **predates** the Compact's entry into force — postal
  autonomy came first. StampWorldHistory dates the trusteeship 1947–1990.
- Under the Compact the **USPS still transports FSM mail**, FSM remains in the
  **US ZIP system**, and **US domestic rates apply**. This is why Scott lists
  FSM fully and why FSM stamps are functionally US-adjacent.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers — catalogue names

**This is the core disambiguation section.** All four points below were checked.

### (a) German Caroline Islands — a SEPARATE catalogue country ✅ CONFIRMED
- Scott: **"Caroline Islands"** — approx. **23 major numbers** in the Scott
  Classic catalogue (1899–1915). Confidence: Medium–High.
- Michel: **"Karolinen"**, within German Colonies (Deutsche Kolonien).
- StampWorld: **"German Caroline Islands"**, 1899–1915.
- **Do NOT merge with FSM.** Different country, different numbering, different
  era, wildly different forgery profile.
- Sister German colonial issuers: **Marianen** (Marianas) and
  **Marshall-Inseln** (Marshall Islands) — also separate catalogue countries.

### (b) Japanese mandate — NO separate stamps ✅ CONFIRMED (the trap)
Wikipedia (Caroline Islands), verbatim:
> *"Japan occupied the islands in October 1914, after which Japanese stamps
> were used until the end of World War II."*

Corroborated by the Marshall Islands article: *"stamps of Japan were used from
1914 to 1944."*

**There is no "South Seas Mandate" or "Nan'yō" catalogue country.** Japan
administered the mandate using ordinary Japanese stamps. Material from this
period is collected as **Japanese stamps used in the Carolines** — i.e. the
philatelic interest is entirely in the **postmarks/cancellations**, not the
stamps. Any dealer listing offering "Nan'yō mandate stamps" as a distinct
issuer is misdescribing Japanese stamps.

### (c) 1944–1984 TTPI — UNITED STATES stamps, no catalogue country ✅ CONFIRMED
Wikipedia (FSM), verbatim:
> *"The islands then became part of the United Nations Trust Territory of the
> Pacific and used American stamps from 1946."*

Wikipedia (Caroline Islands): the islands *"used U.S. stamps until 1983."*

**There is no Scott/SG/Michel country for the Trust Territory of the Pacific
Islands.** This is a ~40-year gap in which the collectible material is **US
stamps with TTPI-area cancellations** (Ponape, Truk, Yap, Kosrae, Saipan,
Majuro), plus postal stationery. Again: **cancellation-driven, not stamp-driven.**

### (d) Parallel successors — the three-way 1983/84 split

The TTPI fragmented into three separate stamp-issuing entities:

| Successor | First issue | Confidence | Philatelic agent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Palau** | **1983** (exact date uncertain; postal-independence/inauguration issue, Captain Wilson's 1783 voyage bicentennial, se-tenant 2 blocks of 4; printer **House of Questa**, designer Rosemary De Figlio) | Year High; exact date Uncertain | **IGPC** (later) |
| **Marshall Islands** | **1984** (exact date uncertain; brief cited "May 1984" — **could not verify**) | Year High; month/day **Uncertain** | **Unicover Corp.**, Cheyenne, Wyoming |
| **Micronesia (FSM)** | **12 July 1984** | Medium–High | **IGPC**, New York |

Palau therefore **preceded** FSM and the Marshalls by roughly a year.
**Northern Mariana Islands** did NOT take postal independence — it became a US
Commonwealth and continues to use US stamps, so there is no CNMI catalogue country.

## 5. Inscriptions (text printed on the stamps)

| Period | Inscription | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Spanish Carolines | *(no stamps issued)* | High |
| German colonial 1899–1900 | **KAROLINEN** overprinted on German Eagle/Reichspost types | High |
| German colonial 1901–1915 | **KAROLINEN** + **DEUTSCHES REICH** on the Kaiser's Yacht (SMY *Hohenzollern*) design | High |
| Japanese mandate 1914–1944 | **Japanese stamps** — inscriptions are 大日本帝國郵便 etc. **No Caroline/Micronesian inscription of any kind** | High |
| US TTPI 1944–1984 | **US stamps** — inscribed "UNITED STATES POSTAGE" / "USA". **No distinctive TTPI inscription exists.** Identification is by **cancellation only** | High |
| FSM 1984– | **"FEDERATED STATES OF MICRONESIA"** (most common, esp. early/formal issues) and **"MICRONESIA"** (shorter form, widely used on later thematic issues) | High |

**Practical OCR/recognition note.** "MICRONESIA" alone is **ambiguous** —
"Micronesia" is also the name of the wider Pacific subregion encompassing Palau,
the Marshalls, Kiribati, Nauru and Guam. A stamp inscribed only "MICRONESIA"
is FSM, but the word appearing in *text* about a stamp may not be. Treat
**"FEDERATED STATES OF MICRONESIA"** as the high-confidence token and bare
**"MICRONESIA"** as needing corroboration (currency in US cents, ZIP-adjacent
design cues, date ≥1984).

Also note **KAROLINEN** is unambiguous and is the single best token for the
German-era country.

## 6. Gaps — years with no issues

| Gap | Length | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-1899** | — | Spain administered the Carolines but **never issued stamps or even postmarks** for them. There is no Spanish Carolines philatelic material of the ordinary kind. |
| **1915–1984** | **~69 years** | The single defining feature of this country's philately. |

Broken down:
- **1915–1944 (~30 yrs)** — Japanese occupation/mandate. Japanese stamps used.
- **1944–1984 (~40 yrs)** — US Navy administration then TTPI. US stamps used.

**Between the last German Karolinen issue (1915) and the first FSM issue
(12 July 1984) there are no stamps inscribed for these islands at all.** Nearly
seven decades. Any item claiming to be a "Caroline Islands" or "Micronesia"
stamp dated within that window is either (a) a mislabelled Japanese or US stamp,
(b) a local/revenue/cinderella label, or (c) **bogus**. This gap is a strong
authentication filter — use it.

Minor gap: within the German period, issues cluster at 1899–1901 and then
sparse emergency surcharges (1905, 1910); WWI ended the colony in Oct 1914.

## 7. Forgery risk and "wallpaper" risk

### 7a. German Caroline Islands (1899–1900) — SEVERE forgery risk ✅ CONFIRMED

The brief's claim that these are among the most forged stamps in philately is
**well supported.**

- Wikipedia/dealer consensus: *"Forgeries abound in most issues of the German
  Caroline Islands, especially the overprints."*
- The **German Philatelic Society Reference Manual of Forgeries** documents
  **four distinct forged overprints** for the 1900 overprinted issue
  (Michel Karolinen 1–6). **This is a formal, citable reference — not a forum
  claim.**
- **Two forged surcharges** documented for the 1910 5pf-on-3pf.
- **François Fournier** — the notorious Geneva forger — produced forgeries of
  both the **Karolinen overprints** and the **1901 Kaiser's Yacht** series
  (5pf, 10pf, 40pf, 50pf, 80pf documented). Fournier also produced
  **fraudulent postmarks**, including a forged Jaluit line cancel.

**The economic driver — the 48° vs 56° overprint:**

| Variety | Angle | Year | Scott | Value |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| First overprint | **48°** | 1899 | **1a–6a** | scarce — "$60+ at least" |
| Second overprint | **56°** | 1900 | **1–6** | common — modest |

The overprint angle **alone** separates a cheap stamp from an expensive one,
and the two are otherwise **physically identical**. This is the ideal forgery
economics: take a common 56° stamp (or a plain German stamp) and apply a fake
48° overprint. **Every 48° "1899" Karolinen must be treated as suspect until
expertised.** Note the counterintuitive Scott convention: the **rarer** 1899
issue carries the **lettered minor** numbers (1a–6a).

Extreme rarity to be aware of: **1910 5pf-on-3pf brown handstamp surcharge,
Scott catalogue ~$5,500.** Anything at this value level attracts forgery.

**Recommendation: for German Karolinen, require expertisation (BPP or
equivalent) for anything beyond the common 56° Yacht types.**

### 7b. FSM modern issues — "wallpaper" reputation: DESERVED, but they are NOT illegal

This distinction matters and is routinely botched. Two separate questions:

**(i) Is FSM an agency issuer producing excessive thematic material? YES — evidenced.**

- **The agency is IGPC — Inter-Governmental Philatelic Corporation**, New York
  (460 W. 34th St). Documented: IGPC's *"range of customers grew to include the
  newly sovereign Federated States of Micronesia, which led to the acquisition
  of more Pacific Island clients, such as the Marshall Islands, Palau, Papua
  New Guinea, Samoa, Tuvalu, and Vanuatu."* **FSM was IGPC's gateway client for
  the Pacific.** Confidence: Medium–High (Washington Post 1990 + IGPC's own
  corporate material).

- ⚠️ **Do NOT credit Unicover for FSM.** **Unicover Corp. (Cheyenne, Wyoming)
  was the agent for the MARSHALL ISLANDS**, not FSM. The two are constantly
  conflated because both are TTPI successors that went independent in 1984.
  **FSM → IGPC. Marshalls → Unicover.** (Printing is a third axis again: the
  **House of Questa**, London, printed FSM stamps in 1990 and Palau's 1983
  first issue.)

- **The specialist verdict.** The **Pacific Islands Study Circle** published
  Terry Commins' *Catalogue of Stamps of the Federated States of Micronesia*
  (2020, 75pp) which deliberately **stops at 1990**, because after that
  *"commercial dictates began an issuing policy bearing little relation to
  local culture or issues."* **This is the strongest evidence available** — a
  serious specialist society drawing an explicit line at 1990 and declining to
  catalogue what came after. Confidence: High.

- StampWorldHistory, more neutrally: FSM issues *"a blend of issues with themes
  of national interest, and a significant number of issues aimed at the
  thematic collectors market."*

- **Practical dividing line: ~1990.** Pre-1990 FSM = modest, locally relevant,
  taken seriously by specialists. Post-1990 = agency-driven thematic sheetlets
  (Disney-adjacent, Princess Diana, Pokémon-era pop culture, dinosaurs, etc.),
  heavy on multi-design souvenir sheets.

**(ii) Is FSM on any UPU illegal-issues list? NO EVIDENCE FOUND — stated plainly.**

I searched the UPU philatelic circulars framework and the **Philatelic
Webmasters Organization illegal-issues summary**, and checked the country list
directly:

> **Micronesia does NOT appear.** The countries named there include Afghanistan,
> Angola, Azerbaijan, Benin, Burkina Faso, Burundi, Comoros, Congo (both),
> Côte d'Ivoire, Cyprus, Chad, East Timor, Estonia, Georgia, Guinea,
> Kyrgyzstan, Laos, Madagascar, Mali, Mongolia, Morocco, Myanmar, Niger,
> Rwanda, Russian Federation, Senegal, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Ukraine,
> UAE, Uzbekistan.

**I could not find any UPU circular naming FSM, and I am not going to imply one
exists.** On present evidence:

- FSM stamps are **legitimate, government-authorised, postally valid** issues of
  a UPU member state.
- **Scott lists them** — Scott's policy is to list validly issued stamps, and
  Scott's willingness to catalogue FSM in full is itself evidence of legitimacy.
- "Wallpaper" is an **aesthetic and investment** criticism (excessive,
  collector-targeted, poor secondary-market value), **not a legality
  criticism**. The two must not be conflated.

**Bottom line for a grading/scanning system:** FSM modern issues are genuine but
**low-value and high-volume**. The realistic risk is **overvaluation**, not
counterfeiting — there is little economic incentive to forge a stamp worth
under a dollar. **Forgery risk concentrates almost entirely in the German
Karolinen era.** Inverted risk profile: old = forgery risk, new = valuation risk.

### Copyright status — a genuine split

- **German Karolinen (1899–1915): PUBLIC DOMAIN.** Safe to use. Age alone
  clears them, and German government works of this vintage are unencumbered.
- **Japanese-mandate and TTPI-era material:** the *stamps* are Japanese and US
  issues — US stamps pre-1978 are largely PD; **Japanese stamps of the period
  are PD by age.** But the collectible element is the *cancellation*, which
  means you need a photograph of a cover — and the **photograph** may carry its
  own copyright.
- **FSM modern issues (1984– ): ASSUME COPYRIGHTED.** Agency-produced designs
  (IGPC), and many post-1990 thematics carry **third-party licensed IP**
  (Disney characters, sports bodies, film franchises). These are **doubly
  encumbered** — postal-administration copyright *plus* licensed character IP.
  **Do not reproduce without permission.** This is the single biggest image-
  sourcing constraint for this country.

**Recommendation:** source German Karolinen freely; for modern FSM, either
license properly, use user-photographed images with clear provenance, or rely
on text-only catalogue data.

## 9. Uncertain — explicit list

Being generous here, per the brief:

1. **Total Scott major numbers for FSM.** Not verified. My ~1,000–1,400 estimate
   is inference, not a source. **Needs a physical Scott Volume 4.**
2. **Exact first-issue date.** 12 July 1984 (two sources) vs. the brief's
   10 July. **12 July is better supported but not conclusive.**
3. **Scott 1–4 for the first issue.** Consistent with everything found, never
   directly confirmed.
4. **"Scott 1–66, C1–C38 for 1984–1988."** Search-summary only.
5. **Printer of the 1984 first issue.** Completely unknown. Questa is a
   *guess by association* — resist it.
6. **Palau's exact 1983 first-issue date.** Year only.
7. **Marshall Islands' exact 1984 first-issue date.** "May 1984" appeared in
   the brief; **I could not corroborate it.**
8. **Stanley Gibbons count.** No figure found at all.
9. **TTPI start year** — 1944 vs 1946 vs 1947; sources genuinely differ
   depending on whether they mean military occupation or formal trusteeship.
10. **Colnect's 2,496.** Snippet-derived; page unreachable.
11. **Whether Scott lists literally every FSM issue.** Scott's general policy
    suggests yes, but I found no explicit statement, and Scott does sometimes
    place excessive issues in appendices or decline listing.
12. **Michel's exact FSM range.** Only the single Millennium data point
    (947–963) is confirmed.
13. **Caroline Islands "23 major numbers"** — from a blog citing the 2011 Scott
    Classic; may not match current Scott.
14. **Whether any FSM issue has ever been individually denounced.** None found,
    but absence of evidence in a limited web search is not proof.
15. **The 48°/56° angle figures** are widely repeated; I have not confirmed them
    against a Michel/Scott technical description.
