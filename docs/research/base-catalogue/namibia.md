# Namibia

Base catalogue skeleton. Research date: 2026-07-19.

**Critical structural note for the database:** almost all pre-1990 material collected as
"Namibia" is catalogued by every major catalogue under a *different country name* ‚Äî either
**German South West Africa** or **South West Africa**. Namibia proper begins in 1990.
See ¬ß4.

**Which to trust.** For a working skeleton, use the **StampWorld ~1,039 for Namibia
1990‚Äì2025** and **~682 for South West Africa 1923‚Äì1990**, flagged as provisional. StampWorld
is a free aggregator, not an editorially rigorous catalogue: it tends to list minor varieties
and sheetlet components as separate numbers, so it generally runs **higher** than Scott or
Stanley Gibbons for the same period. Expect a true Scott major-number count for Namibia to be
meaningfully lower ‚Äî likely in the 800‚Äì1,000 band ‚Äî but this is an inference, **not a verified
figure**, and must not be entered as data.

The ~1,084 vs ~1,145 spread between two aggregator databases for overlapping periods is itself
evidence of how unstable these counts are. **No Scott, SG, Michel or Yvert count for Namibia
was verified in this pass.** Do not populate a count field from an unnamed catalogue.

Note the significance of the **1,039 / 682 split**: roughly 60% of the "Namibia region" corpus
sits under the *South West Africa* heading, not *Namibia*.

## 2. First issue

**Namibia (own first issue)**

- **Date:** 21 March 1990 ‚Äî Namibian independence day. Verified across multiple sources.
- **Type:** Independence commemorative set.
- **Catalogue number:** **not verified.** A Scott number was not confirmed. Do not guess.
- **Subject / printer:** One source (Grokipedia, an **AI-generated encyclopedia**) gives
  designer Theo Marais, printing by the Government Printer in Pretoria, denominations
  18c / 45c / 60c, perf 14¬º x 14, ~1 million sets. **This is exactly the class of AI-generated
  attribution the brief warns about and it was NOT corroborated by any independent source.**
  Treat as unverified. See ¬ß9.

**Predecessor entities' first issues**

- **German South West Africa:** **May 1897** ‚Äî German Empire stamps overprinted
  `Deutsch- / S√ºdwest-Afrika` at an angle (hyphenated form). Overprint wording changed
  **15 November 1898** to `Deutsch- / S√ºdwestafrika` (hyphen dropped). Verified via Wikipedia.
  Exact day in May 1897 not verified. Printer not verified.
- **South West Africa:** **1 January 1923** ‚Äî first stamps inscribed "South West Africa",
  produced by overprinting Union of South Africa stamps, English and Afrikaans alternating
  through the sheet. Verified.

A 3 Mark Yacht value was **printed in 1919 but never placed on sale in the colony** ‚Äî a
printing event, not an issue. Catalogue treatment varies; flag rather than assume.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers ‚Äî catalogue headings

Names a catalogue would actually use:

1. **German South West Africa** (Michel: *Deutsch-S√ºdwestafrika*; Yvert: *Sud-Ouest Africain
   Allemand*) ‚Äî 1897‚Äì1919.
2. **1915 South African occupation** ‚Äî generally **not a separate catalogue country**. South
   African stamps circulated unoverprinted 1915‚Äì1922, so this period is catalogued under
   *South Africa*, not under SWA. This is a common cataloguing trap.
3. **South West Africa** (also *Sud-Ouest Africain* / *S√ºdwestafrika*) ‚Äî 1923‚Äì1990.
4. **Namibia** ‚Äî 1990‚Äì .

**The 1968 renaming does NOT create a catalogue break.** UN General Assembly Resolution
2372 (XXII), adopted **12 June 1968**, renamed the territory Namibia. South Africa rejected
this and continued administering it as South West Africa. **Stamps continued to be inscribed
"South West Africa" / "SWA" right up to 1990.** Catalogues therefore keep the *South West
Africa* heading until 1990 ‚Äî the 1968 date has philatelic relevance only as background, and a
database must not split the country at 1968. This is verified and important.

**Bilingual pairs ‚Üí SWA-only:** the transition is **1970**, not 1968. The `SWA` abbreviation
existed much earlier as a 1927 overprint device, so "SWA" text alone does not date a stamp to
post-1970. Beware conflating the two. The **exact final year of full bilingual
`South West Africa / Suidwes-Afrika` inscriptions was not verified** ‚Äî sources say bilingual
usage ran through the 1950s and that SWA became general in 1970, leaving the 1953‚Äì1970 window
imprecisely documented. See ¬ß9.

Post-1990, Afrikaans was dropped from **postmarks**, which became English-only.

## 6. Gaps

- **1915 ‚Äì 31 December 1922 ‚Äî no territorial issues.** After the South African Army overran
  German South West Africa in 1914‚Äì15, unoverprinted Union of South Africa stamps were used.
  This is a genuine ~8-year gap in the country's own catalogue listing, ended by the
  1 Jan 1923 overprints. Verified.
- **1919‚Äì1922** additionally covers the post-Versailles interregnum before the League of
  Nations C-mandate arrangements were bedded down.
- **1989‚Üí1990 is not a gap** ‚Äî issuing was continuous. The final SWA definitive set (1989,
  15 minerals/mining stamps) was largely re-used for Namibia in 1990 with the country name
  changed, cuprite dropped, willemite added, and a chemical-equation error on the boltwoodite
  stamp corrected. A useful and verified detail for de-duplicating near-identical designs.
- No other multi-year gaps identified. Absence of evidence, not evidence of absence.

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

**Wallpaper / agency risk: LOW. Namibia is NOT a wallpaper issuer.**

- Stamps are issued by **NamPost (Namibia Post Limited)**, a UPU-recognised designated
  operator with its own philatelic bureau and published stamp programme.
- NamPost has **reduced** its programme to roughly **three issues per year, ~12 individual
  stamps** ‚Äî the opposite of agency behaviour. Verified from NamPost's own philately pages.
- **No evidence found** linking Namibia to IGPC, Stamperija, or any comparable agency, and
  **no UPU illegal-issue circular naming Namibia was found.** Note this is a negative finding
  from a limited search, not an exhaustive UPU records audit.
- CTO remainder practice by NamPost: **not verified either way.** Packet-mixture Namibian
  material exists on the market but its origin was not established.

**Forgery risk on German South West Africa: HIGH.**

- **Forged cancellations are the dominant risk.** Wikipedia states plainly that many used
  GSWA stamps carry forged cancels, and identifies the economic motive: for the high-value
  watermarked Yacht issues, **genuinely used examples are worth up to 10x mint**. Windhuk
  and other desirable colonial cancels are therefore prime forgery targets. Any used GSWA
  high value should be treated as guilty until expertised.
- **Forged overprints.** The 1897/1898 issues are overprints on ordinary, cheap, widely
  available German definitives ‚Äî the classic forger's setup. Forgeries of German colonial
  overprints were appearing **as early as 1903**.
- **MYTH FLAGGED ‚Äî the "Vineta provisional" is NOT a German South West Africa stamp.**
  The brief lists it under GSWA. It is not. The Vineta provisional was created on
  **13 April 1901 aboard the cruiser SMS Vineta**, off **Pernambuco, Brazil**, when the ship's
  postal officer bisected 5 Pfennig Germania stamps and handstamped them "3 PF"; the mail went
  to Germany on 17 April 1901. It is a **German Empire** item (Michel briefly listed it as
  no. 67, then withdrew the number as it was widely judged a self-made variety rather than an
  official Reichspost issue). ~600 produced. It has **no connection to South West Africa** and
  must not be catalogued under Namibia or GSWA. It is heavily forged ‚Äî but that is a German
  Empire problem, not a Namibian one.

**Modern Namibia forgery risk: low.** No significant forgery literature found.

## 8. Image sourcing

- **German South West Africa (1897‚Äì1919) ‚Äî likely public domain.** Wikimedia Commons maintains
  `Template:PD-German Empire stamps`, covering stamps of the Deutsche Reichspost issued before
  1945 **including colonial entities**. GSWA falls within this. Commons has
  `Category:German South-West Africa` and related stamp categories. **Best source for the
  colonial era.** Verify the specific template on each file rather than assuming.
- **South West Africa (1923‚Äì1990) ‚Äî mixed / caution.** Commons has
  `Category:Postage stamps of South West Africa` and `Category:Stamps of South West Africa`.
  Commons' own Africa guidance indicates SWA-era material may fall under **South African**
  government copyright rules rather than Namibian law. South African government works carry a
  50-year term, so mid-century SWA issues are plausibly expired ‚Äî but per-file verification is
  required. **Do not bulk-assume PD for this era.**
- **Namibia (1990‚Äì ) ‚Äî under copyright.** Namibia's *Copyright and Neighbouring Rights
  Protection Act 6 of 1994* vests copyright in government-produced works in the state for
  **50 years after publication**. So 1990 issues would not begin entering the public domain
  until ~2040. Modern NamPost issues are **not** freely reusable; licence from NamPost or use
  own photography under an applicable exception.
- Commons' Namibia entry also references a **12 June 1968** cutoff ‚Äî which is the UN renaming
  date, not an independence date (independence is 21 March 1990). The page's phrasing appeared
  imprecise in the fetched summary; **re-read the Commons Namibia entry directly before
  relying on it.**
