# Korea (North) — Democratic People's Republic of Korea

**Date:** 19 July 2026 · **Purpose:** base-catalogue dossier. The DPRK is simultaneously one of
the highest-volume issuers in the world and one of the least trustworthy, and almost everything
"everyone knows" about it is a half-truth. This dossier tries to separate the documented from
the folkloric.

---

## 1. Headline counts

⚠️ **A count without a named catalogue is meaningless here, and the DPRK is the worst case in
the entire corpus** — because the country's *own* catalogue (KSC) and the Western catalogues
disagree not just on granularity but on **whether certain items were issued at all**.

| Scope | Count | Catalogue / source | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Postage stamps, 1946–2026 | **7,571** | **StampWorld**, "North Korea" | Medium — read from a search index, not the page (stampworld.com 403s to automated fetch) |
| Stamps 1946 – Nov 2015 | **all DPRK issues in 610 pages** | **Korean Stamp Catalogue (조선우표목록)**, 10th ed. 2016, Korea Stamp Corporation | High as to scope, **no item total obtained** |
| Scott | **listed, range unknown** | Scott (Vol. 4 / Korea) | Scott 1 and Scott 5 are attested for 1946; **the modern high number was not established** |
| Michel | **listed** in *Mittel- und Ostasien* | Michel | Michel 1/2, 3/4, 5/6, 7 attested for the 1946 issues; **no total** |
| Stanley Gibbons | listed in *Part 21 (China)* | SG | **not verified** |

**Which I trust more, and why.** For *existence and numbering of what the DPRK considers its
own issues*, the **Korean Stamp Catalogue (KSC)** is authoritative — it is the issuer's own
catalogue and Korea Stamp Society members cite KSC numbers (e.g. KSC #454, #998, #1022–1028) as
the working reference. But KSC is a *political* document as well as a philatelic one: it has
retroactively added, relabelled and re-imaged items across editions (see §7), so it is
authoritative about intent rather than about history.

For a **base catalogue skeleton, key on Scott or Michel and carry KSC as a cross-reference.**
StampWorld's 7,571 is the only usable order-of-magnitude figure and should be treated as an
**upper bound** — it will fold in sheetlets, imperf variants and the CTO-only material that
Scott and SG suppress or footnote. Expect Scott to land materially lower, plausibly in the
**5,000–6,500** band. **Do not publish that band as a fact — it is an inference, not a source.**

⚠️ **The DPRK "outproduced South Korea from the 1970s"** (Wikipedia). Whatever the exact total,
the shape is not in dispute: a small, poor, isolated country with modest domestic mail volumes
is one of the world's most prolific stamp issuers. That gap between postal need and stamp output
is the whole story of this country's philately.

## 2. First issue

| Field | Value | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Date** | **12 March 1946** | High — Wikipedia, Korea Stamp Society, StampWorld all agree |
| **Subjects** | **Rose of Sharon** (mugunghwa, 무궁화) and **Samson Rock** (Diamond Mountains / Kumgangsan) | High |
| **Catalogue nos.** | **Scott 1** and **Scott 5** are attested for the 1946 issues; **Michel 1/2, 3/4, 5/6, 7** | Medium |
| **Issuing authority** | **Provisional People's Committee of North Korea** (est. 8–9 Feb 1946, chaired by Kim Il Sung), under the **Soviet Civil Administration** | High |
| **Printer** | **NOT ESTABLISHED** — see §9 | — |
| **Currency** | **Korean yen** — the DPRK continued the colonial-era yen until the won was introduced in Dec 1947 | High |

⚠️ **The first issue predates the state by two and a half years.** The DPRK was not proclaimed
until **9 September 1948**. The 1946–48 stamps were issued by a Soviet-supervised provisional
committee, not by a sovereign DPRK. Catalogues nevertheless open the "Korea (North)" country run
at 1946. **This is a country key that begins before its country does** — model it as an
authority change within one key, not as a separate issuer, or you will fight every catalogue.

⚠️ **Myth check — "the first DPRK stamp shows Kim Il Sung."** **False.** The first issue is a
flower and a rock formation. Kim Il Sung portraiture on stamps is later. The claim appears to
come from back-projecting the personality cult onto the founding issue.

**Note on 1946–48 material:** the Soviet-occupation issues are **genuinely scarce** and command
high prices. This is the one part of DPRK philately where scarcity is real rather than
manufactured — which makes it the one part with a serious forgery incentive (§7).

## 3. Issuing periods

| Period | Issuing authority (catalogue-facing) | Catalogue country key |
|---|---|---|
| 1884 – 1905 | **Korea** — Chosun (Joseon) Dynasty / Korean Empire | **Korea** (a distinct, pre-partition key) |
| 1905 – 15 Aug 1945 | **Japan** — the Korean postal service was absorbed by Japan in 1905 and Korea annexed in 1910. **No Korea-specific stamps.** Japanese stamps used throughout. | **Japan** |
| 24 Aug 1945 – 1946 | **Soviet Civil Administration** — no stamps of its own | — |
| **12 Mar 1946 – 8 Sep 1948** | **Provisional People's Committee of North Korea**, under Soviet Civil Administration | **Korea (North)** |
| 9 Sep 1948 – present | **Democratic People's Republic of Korea**; philatelic operations run by the **Korea Stamp Corporation (조선우표사)**, Pyongyang | **Korea (North)** |

**Programme phases that matter more than the political dates:**

- **1946 – c.1974 — domestic-facing.** Subjects are Korean: leaders, the Party, reconstruction,
  Korean landscape, patriotic and revolutionary themes. Broadly congruent with actual postal use.
- **c.1974 – present — export-facing.** Wikipedia is explicit: **"After 1974, in a bid to bring
  more hard currency into the country, North Korea began issuing stamps with wide-ranging
  themes."** Joan of Arc, airships, sports, wildlife, Princess Diana, Disney-adjacent art,
  European royalty. **Many have no connection to North Korea whatsoever.** The 1974 pivot is the
  single most useful date in DPRK philately — it separates stamps made for Koreans from stamps
  made for foreigners' albums.
- **Late 1977 onward** — English **"DPR Korea"** starts appearing, a direct consequence of the
  export orientation. See §5; this is a hard dating marker.
- **1965** — DPRK joins the **Fédération Internationale de Philatélie (FIP)**, 15 June 1965.

## 4. Predecessor / successor issuers (catalogue names)

```
Korea (Chosun Dynasty / Korean Empire, 1884–1905)
        │
        ├──1905/1910──▶ Japan  (no Korea-specific issues, 1905–1945)
        │
        └──1945 partition──┬──▶ Korea (North)   1946–  ← this dossier
                           └──▶ Korea (South)   1946–
```

**Predecessors, in the names a catalogue would use:**
1. **Japan** (1905–1945) — the immediate predecessor issuer for the territory. Forty years of
   Korean postal history sits under **Japan** and is invisible to any "Korea" search.
2. **Korea** (1884–1905) — the Chosun Dynasty / Korean Empire key. Shared ancestor with South Korea.

**Sibling:** **Korea (South)**, from 1946. Not a successor — a co-successor. The 1945 split is a
**fork**, and must be modelled as one edge in, two edges out.

**Also worth wiring:**
- **US Army Military Government in Korea (USAMGIK)** — the southern counterpart of the Soviet
  Civil Administration; relevant only as the mirror of the North's 1945–48 arrangement.
- **Soviet Civil Administration** — an authority, **not** an issuer. Do not create a country key
  for it; it issued no stamps of its own.

⚠️ **Do NOT succeed "Korea" straight to "Korea (South)".** A common and wrong simplification,
usually because South Korea is the internationally familiar "Korea". Both Koreas descend equally
from the 1884–1905 Korea key.

## 5. Inscriptions — the strongest identification field this country has

The DPRK's inscriptions changed in ways that date a stamp almost mechanically. The Korea Stamp
Society's identification guide gives the two decisive tests:

### Test 1 — reading direction of the hangul (dates 1946–47 vs 1948+)

| Period | Printed text | Reading direction | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1946 – 1947** | **죠선우표** — rendered on the stamps as **표우선죠** | **RIGHT TO LEFT** | Uses the archaic syllable **죠** (*jyo*) |
| **1948 – present** | **조선우표** (*Chosŏn up'yo*, "Korean stamp") | **LEFT TO RIGHT** | Uses the modern syllable **조** (*cho*) |

**Two changes coincide at 1948: the direction flips *and* 죠 becomes 조.** Either one alone dates
a stamp to the Soviet-occupation era. This is the most reliable early-DPRK marker in existence
and is worth encoding as a first-class field.

### Test 2 — English text (dates pre- vs post-1977)

- **No English at all** before late 1977.
- **"DPR Korea"** begins appearing **late 1977**. Also seen: **"DPRK"**, and the full
  **"DEMOCRATIC PEOPLE'S REPUBLIC OF KOREA"**.
- ⚠️ **"North Korea" NEVER appears on a DPRK stamp.** The state does not call itself that.
  Any stamp inscribed "North Korea" is not a DPRK issue.
- ⚠️ **The DPRK never inscribes "Korea" in English alone.** South Korea does. This is a clean
  discriminator.

### Other inscriptions

- **Country/state name in hangul:** **조선민주주의인민공화국** (*Chosŏn Minjujuŭi Inmin
  Konghwaguk*) on many issues. Note the DPRK self-designation is **조선 (Chosŏn)**, whereas South
  Korea uses **한국 (Hanguk)** — a lexical split, not just a script one.
- **Currency:** **전 (chŏn)** and **원 (wŏn)**. Pre-Dec 1947 issues are denominated in the
  **Korean yen**, the colonial holdover.
- **Script:** **hangul throughout**, essentially never hanja on DPRK issues (the DPRK abolished
  hanja in general use). Hanja on a "Korean" stamp points to the Chosun Dynasty era or to
  South Korea, not to the DPRK.

### Distinguishing from the pre-partition Chosun Dynasty (1884–1905)

Those stamps spell the country **"COREAN" / "COREA"** in Latin script and give values in English
as **mun** and **poon**. Latin "COREA" ⇒ dynasty era, never DPRK.

## 6. Gaps

The DPRK's gap profile is the **inverse** of the other three countries in this batch: its problem
is not silence, it is noise. But there are real gaps.

| Years | Status | Cause |
|---|---|---|
| 1905 – 1945 | **No Korean stamps at all** (40 years) | Japanese annexation; Japanese stamps used. Catalogued under **Japan**. |
| Aug 1945 – Mar 1946 | **No stamps** (~7 months) | Interregnum between Japanese surrender and the Provisional People's Committee's first issue. |
| **1950 – 1953** | **Sharply reduced output; issues exist but are scarce and irregular** | Korean War. Printing and distribution were disrupted; surviving material is genuinely rare and the record is thin. ⚠️ **I could not establish whether any calendar year in this window is wholly empty.** Do not encode a hard gap here without checking. |
| 1974 – present | **No gaps — the opposite** | Continuous high-volume output. |

⚠️ **Gaps in the *catalogue* are not gaps in *issuing*.** KSC numbers 1022–1028 sat listed as
"unissued" with no images through the 1992–1998 editions, then appeared in the 2007 edition
captioned "Improvement of People's Living Standard (Unissued)" **with images shown for the first
time**. The gap was in the published record, not in the printing. Expect more of this.

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

This is the section the DPRK exists to complicate. Four distinct problems, routinely conflated.

### Problem 1 — Wallpaper / export issues: REAL, and documented from the source

**North Korea IS a wallpaper issuer, and unusually it is one by its own admission.** The
mechanism is on the record: after 1974 the DPRK deliberately widened its themes **to earn hard
currency from foreign collectors**. This is not a foreign agency exploiting a poor country's name
(the Stamperija/IGPC model) — **it is the state's own philatelic bureau, the Korea Stamp
Corporation, running the programme itself.** That distinction matters for the database: DPRK
export issues are *legitimate* stamps of a *legitimate* postal administration that were
nonetheless never intended to carry mail. They are not illegal issues, and should not be filed
with illegal issues.

**Encode the 1974 boundary.** Pre-1974 DPRK stamps are postal artefacts. Post-1974 topicals are
export product. Same country, different animal.

### Problem 2 — CTO: the folklore is roughly right, but sloppier than the truth

**What is established:**
- Koryo Tours (a DPRK-specialist operator, not a philatelic authority but a first-hand one):
  **"The majority of DPRK stamps found both inside and outside the country fall into this
  category" [CTO].** Note "**and inside**" — the CTO material is not purely an export
  phenomenon.
- The value hierarchy dealers actually apply: **genuinely postally used > mint > CTO.** That
  ordering is itself evidence — genuinely used DPRK material is scarcer than mint, which is
  the signature of an issuer whose stamps mostly never franked anything.
- 1980s onward is the mass-CTO era; 1950s–70s material is scarce and sought after.

**What the folklore overstates:**
- ⚠️ **"DPRK stamps were never sold domestically" is FALSE as a general claim.** Stamps are and
  were sold at post offices, at the Korea Stamp Museum in Pyongyang and at Pyongyang gift shops;
  visitors buy them and post mail with them. Korea Stamp Society discussion documents specific
  stamps that "were used in the post offices and have normal postage cancellation."
- ⚠️ The accurate claim is much narrower: **certain individual issues** had tiny domestic
  circulation, or were withdrawn, or were distributed abroad in configurations different from
  those sold at home. A documented example: for **KSC #454**, a collector records that
  **"in the USSR, this set was sold without this stamp"**, that mint copies are almost never
  seen, and that **"almost the whole small circulation of this stamp was probably canceled to
  order."** That is a claim about one stamp, hedged with "probably" — and it is exactly the sort
  of specific, sourced statement that gets flattened into "North Korean stamps are all fake."
- **Verdict:** treat "not sold domestically" as an **item-level flag requiring evidence**, never
  as a country-level property. The country-level property is *CTO-dominant*, which is different
  and defensible.

### Problem 3 — Unissued / irregular material: a real category with an unusual cause

The Korea Stamp Society maintains a taxonomy of "unissued or otherwise irregular" DPRK stamps
(most of it members-only). The distinctive DPRK cause is **political**: stamps get withdrawn
because a depicted person is purged, a slogan is superseded, or a policy is abandoned. Some
withdrawn stamps nonetheless reached collectors. Some catalogued as "issued" are "much rarer in
comparison with other regular stamps," implying minimal real distribution.

**Database consequence:** the DPRK needs an `issued_status` richer than issued/unissued —
minimally *issued / issued-but-withdrawn / unissued-but-distributed / unissued*. And it needs to
record **which KSC edition** a claim comes from, because KSC changes its mind.

### Problem 4 — Forgery: LOW where it is worthless, HIGH where it is not

- **Post-1974 topicals: negligible forgery risk.** Nobody forges a stamp that is abundant and
  cheap. This inverts the usual assumption that a "dodgy" country implies forgery risk — the DPRK
  is dodgy in a way that *removes* the forgery incentive across most of its output.
- **1946–1948 Soviet-occupation issues: HIGH risk.** Genuinely scarce, high prices, simple
  early designs, and a period whose reference literature is thin. This is where to concentrate
  expertisation flags.
- **Korean War-period material: HIGH risk**, for the same reasons plus a chaotic documentary record.
- **Cancellations are the soft spot throughout.** Since genuinely used > CTO in value, the
  profitable fake is not a fake stamp but a **fake postal cancellation on a genuine CTO stamp**.
  Any DPRK stamp whose premium depends on being "genuinely postally used" — especially on cover —
  should be flagged.

### Myths, adjudicated

| Claim | Verdict |
|---|---|
| "North Korean stamps are not real stamps / are illegal issues" | **False.** The DPRK is a UPU member and an FIP member (since 1965). Its stamps are issues of a recognised postal administration, however cynical the programme. |
| "They were never sold in North Korea" | **False as stated.** See Problem 2. True only of specific items. |
| "They are all CTO" | **Overstated but directionally right.** "Majority", per a first-hand source. Not "all". |
| "The first stamp shows Kim Il Sung" | **False.** Rose of Sharon and Samson Rock, 12 March 1946. |
| "North Korea issues more stamps than any other country" | **Unverified.** It outproduced South Korea from the 1970s; that is a much weaker claim than the superlative and is the only version I can source. |
| "Buying them funds the regime / is illegal" | **Partly true, heavily jurisdiction-dependent — see §8.** Not a myth, but not the simple prohibition it is often stated as. |

## 8. Image sourcing

### Copyright — and here there is a genuinely usable finding

**Wikimedia Commons' own territory guidance for North Korea states that North Korean stamps
ARE copyrighted**, under the DPRK Copyright Act (2001; amended 2006, 2012, 2019, 2024). The DPRK
acceded to the **Berne Convention on 28 April 2003**, so its works are protected abroad.

**But the term is short and it has already expired for a large part of the corpus.** Corporate
authorship — "a copyrighted work ... whose author is an institution, enterprise or organization"
— is protected **50 years from publication**. Commons accordingly accepts `{{PD-North Korea}}`
for corporate works **published before 1 January 1976**.

> 🟢 **Practical consequence: DPRK stamps published before 1976 are treatable as public domain
> under DPRK law, which covers the entire domestically-oriented era — 1946 to the 1974 pivot.**
> That is precisely the philatelically interesting portion of this country's output, and it is
> the single most actionable finding in this dossier. The post-1974 export topicals, which are
> the least interesting material, are the part still in copyright.

⚠️ **Caveats before relying on this.** (a) The 50-year term runs from *publication*, so the PD
frontier advances one year at a time — it is not a fixed 1976 line, and by 2026 it reaches 1976
issues. (b) **The rule of the shorter term is not universal**; some jurisdictions may apply a
longer domestic term. (c) DPRK copyright is conditioned on conformity with the socialist order
and Juche, which makes DPRK-law analysis unstable in ways ordinary Berne reasoning does not
capture. (d) Commons separately notes that **government works published on or after 1 February
2006 cannot be used commercially** and are generally not accepted there.

### Sanctions — the real constraint, and it is not copyright

**This is the one country in the corpus where lawful acquisition is a live question.**

- The DPRK is under **one of the most comprehensive US sanctions programmes**, administered by
  OFAC under the **North Korea Sanctions Regulations, 31 CFR Part 510** — a near-total
  prohibition on import and export of goods, services and technology between the US and the DPRK
  for US persons.
- **However, the "informational materials" exemption is directly relevant.** 31 CFR Part 510
  provides that the prohibitions **do not apply to the importation from any country, or
  exportation to any country, of any information or informational materials, whether commercial
  or otherwise, regardless of format or medium** — the Berman Amendment carve-out that runs
  through all OFAC country programmes.
- ⚠️ **Whether postage stamps qualify as "informational materials" is NOT settled** in anything
  I could find. Stamps are plausibly informational — printed matter conveying images and text —
  but they are also *government-issued financial instruments* and, in the DPRK's case,
  explicitly a hard-currency earning device, which cuts the other way. **The sources found do
  not address stamps specifically.**
- **Additional exposure to flag:** buying *new* DPRK stamps from the Korea Stamp Corporation is
  a transaction with a DPRK state entity, which is the thing the sanctions are aimed at.
  Buying a 1950s stamp on a secondary market from a third-country dealer is a materially
  different act. **Age and counterparty matter more than the object.**
- **Non-US jurisdictions differ.** UN Security Council resolutions, EU regulations and others
  impose their own regimes. **Do not generalise from OFAC.**

> 🚨 **Recommendation:** before any commercial use of DPRK stamp imagery or any acquisition
> programme, get a sanctions opinion — this belongs on the same list as the Scott numbering
> question, and it is cheap to ask both at once. **Reproducing an image is a far weaker
> sanctions nexus than importing goods or paying a DPRK entity**, and the informational-materials
> carve-out is likely to cover reproduction; but "likely" is not a basis for a commercial product.

### Where images could lawfully come from

- **Wikimedia Commons: essentially nothing usable.** Consistent with the rest of this batch.
- **`{{PD-North Korea}}` pre-1976 material** — the best route, and it aligns with the most
  valuable era. Verify publication date per item.
- **Own scans of stamps already held** — no import, no DPRK counterparty, no photograph-copyright
  question. Cleanest path by a distance for post-1976 material.
- **University of Chicago Library holds a North Korean Stamp Collection** — an institutional
  holding worth approaching for licensed imagery; terms not established.
- **Korea Stamp Society** — the specialist body; much of its material is members-only, but it is
  the right first contact for anything DPRK.
- ❌ **Do not source from korstamp.com.kp** (the Korea Stamp Corporation's own site) without a
  sanctions opinion. It is a DPRK state entity.

## 9. Uncertain — flagged, not asserted

- **Scott, SG and Michel totals and current high numbers.** None obtained. Only StampWorld's 7,571.
- **The KSC item total.** The 10th edition's 610-page scope is known; the number of catalogued
  stamps is not.
- **The 5,000–6,500 Scott estimate in §1 is an inference from StampWorld's total, not a source.**
  Flagged as such; do not let it harden into a fact.
- **The printer of the 1946 first issue.** Not established. Soviet-zone production is plausible
  but unverified. **Do not guess.**
- **The exact composition of the 12 March 1946 first issue** — how many values, and which Scott
  and Michel numbers map to which design. Scott 1 and Scott 5 are attested for 1946; Michel 1/2,
  3/4, 5/6, 7 are attested; the mapping between them is not.
- **Whether any calendar year 1950–1953 is genuinely empty of issues.**
- **Whether the DPRK is currently a UPU member in good standing**, and its accession date. I did
  not verify it; FIP membership (15 June 1965) is the only international philatelic membership
  I confirmed. **The UPU claim in §7's myth table rests on general knowledge, not on a source
  found here — treat it as the weakest assertion in this dossier.**
- **Whether stamps qualify as "informational materials" under 31 CFR 510.** No source addresses it.
- **DPRK obligations under the rule of the shorter term** in the US, EU and UK specifically.
- **Whether the 1946–47 right-to-left inscription rule holds without exception**, or whether
  transitional 1948 issues exist in both forms.
- **Item-level identification of which DPRK issues were genuinely not sold domestically.** Only
  KSC #454 emerged, and that on a hedged collector's report.
- **The reliability of the KSC as a historical record** across its editions. Known to have
  changed; the pattern of changes is not documented in any source I could reach.
