# Tuvalu

> Research dossier for base-catalogue scoping. Compiled from open web sources, July 2026.
> **Rule applied throughout: accuracy over completeness.** Anything not confirmed against a
> named source is marked "Uncertain" rather than estimated.

---

## 1. Headline counts and the catalogue divergence

Tuvalu is one of the sharpest catalogue-divergence cases in modern Commonwealth philately. The
divergence is **not** primarily about whether Tuvalu is listed at all — both Scott and Stanley
Gibbons do list Tuvalu. It is about **the 1984–1988 individual-island issues**, which Scott
gives full main-listing status and Gibbons relegates to its Appendix of issues considered
issued in excess of postal need.

| Catalogue | Approx. major numbers for "Tuvalu" | Treats 1984–88 island issues as | Notes / confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scott** | Reaches at least **#1468 by 2020** (verified: a 2020 Flowers souvenir sheet is Scott 1468). Total major numbers for Tuvalu proper plausibly **~1,400–1,600** through the early 2020s. | **Full listing**, with a *separate number sequence per island* (e.g. a 1984 Nukufetau set is catalogued Nukufetau #14–19, not a Tuvalu number). | High confidence on 1468 anchor; the total is an inference from it, not a counted figure. |
| **Stanley Gibbons** | Main Tuvalu sequence begins at **SG 1** (1976). Highest current number **Uncertain** — the SG *Western Pacific* catalogue, 4th ed. (2017), carries Tuvalu to **August 2016**. | **Appendix only.** SG's stated reason: *"The following stamps for individual islands of Tuvalu have either been issued in excess of postal needs, or have not been made available to the public in reasonable quantities at face value."* | The Appendix wording is reported second-hand via a Stampboards discussion, not read from the catalogue itself. Treat the *fact* of Appendix relegation as well-attested and the *exact wording* as probable. |
| **Michel / Yvert** | A Michel/Yvert-numbered aggregator (freestampcatalogue) shows **939 stamp *sets*** for Tuvalu — sets, not individual stamps, so not comparable to a Scott major-number count. | Uncertain. | Low confidence; aggregator, not the catalogue. |
| **StampWorld** | **~2,817 items, 1976–2021.** | Uncertain — appears to fold island issues into the Tuvalu country page rather than splitting them. | Medium. StampWorld counts varieties/sheets generously, so this is an *upper bound* style figure, well above Scott majors. |
| **StampData** | **~2,416 items, 1976 onward.** Islands are *not* separate issuers in its model. | Merged into Tuvalu. | Medium. |
| **Colnect** | **Uncertain** — the site is behind bot protection and could not be read. | Uncertain. | — |

**Why the ranges are so wide.** Three different things are being counted: (a) Scott/SG *major
numbers* for Tuvalu proper; (b) those plus the ~800 island-inscribed stamps of 1984–88; and
(c) online databases that additionally enumerate souvenir sheets, se-tenant components and
imperforate variants. A "Tuvalu total" of 1,400, 2,400 or 2,800 can each be defended depending
on which of these you mean. **For a base catalogue, the defensible target is Scott major
numbers for Tuvalu proper (≈1,400–1,600), with the eight island series held as separate
issuer records.**

**Important correction to a common framing:** it is *not* accurate to say Scott and SG "refused
to list" Tuvalu's agency issues. Both list Tuvalu's mainstream output, including the
much-criticised 1984–88 "Leaders of the World" material. The real editorial split is narrower
and specific: **Gibbons appendixed the island-name issues; Scott did not.** Any source claiming
a blanket catalogue boycott of Tuvalu should be treated as unreliable.

## 2. First issue

| Field | Value | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Date** | **1 January 1976** | High — corroborated by Wikipedia (both the Tuvalu and Gilbert & Ellice postal-history articles) and the Tuvalu Philatelic Bureau article. This was the day the Gilbert and Ellice Islands Colony was formally dissolved. |
| **What was issued** | Two things on the same day: (a) a **provisional definitive set** — Gilbert and Ellice Islands **1971 definitives overprinted "TUVALU"**; and (b) a **commemorative set of three stamps**. | High for the overprint provisionals and the 1971 base issue. High that a 3-stamp commemorative accompanied them. |
| **Subject of the 3-stamp commemorative** | **Uncertain.** Sources consistently record "a commemorative set of three" without naming the theme. Do not guess. | — |
| **Catalogue numbers** | The Tuvalu SG sequence begins at **SG 1**; the Scott sequence begins at **Scott 1**. The precise span of the overprint set (commonly cited as SG 1–15 / Scott 1–15, matching the 15-value 1971 G&E definitive series) is **plausible but unverified** — the "15 stamps" figure appears in a StampData 1976 issue grouping, not in a catalogue. | Medium-low on the exact range. |
| **Printer** | **Uncertain.** The G&E 1971 definitives and the 1976 overprinting were done in London — Bradbury Wilkinson and Harrison & Sons are both documented as Western Pacific printers of the era — but **no source consulted attributes the 1976 Tuvalu overprint to a specific firm.** Note that **Format International Security Printers** did *not* start printing for Tuvalu until **1977**, so Format can be excluded for the first issue. | Exclusion of Format is high confidence; positive attribution is unresolved. |
| **First cancellers** | Tuvalu's first postage-stamp cancellation devices entered use the same day, 1 January 1976. | Medium-high. |

**Political vs. philatelic date — a trap.** The Ellice Islands became the separate British colony
of **Tuvalu on 1 October 1975**, but stamps did not change then. Gilbert and Ellice Islands
stamps remained in use until **1 January 1976**. A three-month window exists in which mail from
Tuvalu-the-colony bears Gilbert and Ellice Islands stamps.

| Era | Dates | Issuing authority | Marketing agency | Printer(s) |

| **Tuvalu — agency expansion / "Leaders of the World"** | **1977 – Jan 1987** | Tuvalu Philatelic Bureau | **Philatelists Limited**, later **Philatelists (1980) Limited** | **Format International Security Printers Ltd** (1977–1988) |
| **Tuvalu — PDC period** | **1987 – 1989** | Tuvalu Philatelic Bureau | **Philatelic Distribution Corporation (PDC)** — Clive Feigenbaum | Format International (to 1988, then bankrupt) |
| **Tuvalu — post-scandal / localisation** | **1989 – 2011** | Tuvalu Philatelic Bureau; **Karl Tili** first Tuvaluan general manager, 1989 – 31 Dec 2011 | Transitional; **IGPC** enters at some point (see below) | various |
| **Tuvalu — IGPC period** | **1990s(?) – present** | Tuvalu Post Limited (Bureau restructured; a 2013 proposal considered merging Bureau with the Post Office) | **Inter-Governmental Philatelic Corporation (IGPC)** | IGPC-contracted |

**Which agency, and when — the honest answer.** The 1976–1989 chain (Crown Agents →
Philatelists Ltd → PDC) is **well documented** and dated. The **IGPC start date is not**.
Wikipedia's IGPC article names Tuvalu as a client but the date is unclear in that text; one
reading of it implies 1958, which is **impossible** — Tuvalu did not exist as a stamp issuer
until 1976. A secondary account places IGPC's Pacific expansion (Marshall Islands, Palau,
Samoa, Tuvalu, Vanuatu, PNG) in **the early 1990s**, which is chronologically plausible and
consistent with the 1989 collapse of the previous arrangement — but **treat "early 1990s" as
probable, not established.**

## 4. Predecessor and successor issuers

**Predecessor chain (into Tuvalu):**

1. **Fiji** — Fiji stamps were used in the Gilbert & Ellice Islands before 1911, and the 1911
   protectorate issue was literally Fiji's Edward VII stamps overprinted. *Verified.*
2. **GILBERT AND ELLICE ISLANDS** — catalogue country name. Own issues March 1911 – 1975/76.
   The direct predecessor. *Verified.*
3. **TUVALU** — from 1 January 1976.

**Parallel successor (the other half of the colony):**

- **KIRIBATI** — the Gilbert Islands became independent Kiribati on **12 July 1979**; first
  Kiribati stamps **19 November 1979**. Between 1976 and 1979 the Gilberts issued as
  **"Gilbert Islands"** (a distinct catalogue country from both "Gilbert and Ellice Islands"
  and "Kiribati"). *Verified for the 1979 dates; the intermediate "Gilbert Islands" heading is
  standard catalogue practice and high confidence.*

**The island-name "local" issues — the major catalogue trap:**

- **Eight** islands issued separately, **1984–1988**: **Funafuti, Nanumaga, Nanumea, Niutao,
  Nui, Nukufetau, Nukulaelae, Vaitupu**. Roughly **100 stamps each** (~800 total).
- **Niulakita** (population ~74) is the ninth island and **never had its own issue** — a useful
  negative check against fabricated listings.
- **Note a common briefing error:** the set is *eight* islands, not seven. **Nanumaga** is
  frequently dropped from informal lists. Do not omit it.
- **Catalogue treatment (the trap):**
  - **Scott: full listing**, each island with its **own independent number sequence starting
    at 1**. So "Nukufetau 14–19" is a real Scott citation that has nothing to do with Tuvalu
    #14–19. Any ingest keyed on number alone will collide catastrophically.
  - **Stanley Gibbons: Appendix only**, under the standard excess-of-postal-need formula.
  - **StampWorld / StampData: merged into Tuvalu**, not treated as separate issuers.
  - Consequence: an image or listing labelled "Tuvalu — Vaitupu 1984 #12" is simultaneously a
    valid Scott citation, absent from the SG main catalogue, and unresolvable against a
    Tuvalu-only number space.
- **Recommendation:** model these as **eight distinct issuer records** under a Tuvalu parent,
  with a `catalogue_status` field distinguishing Scott-listed from SG-appendixed.
- The term **"Leeward Islands"** does **not** apply here. Leeward Islands is a separate
  Caribbean catalogue country. Tuvalu's are correctly called *individual-island* or
  *island-name* issues. Any source using "Tuvalu Leeward Islands" is confused or fabricated.

inscription — the colony was always "Gilbert & Ellice".

## 6. Gaps

- **1 Oct 1975 – 31 Dec 1975:** Tuvalu existed as a colony but issued **no stamps**; Gilbert
  and Ellice Islands stamps remained in use. A definitional gap, not a production gap.
- **1989 – early 1990s:** After the PDC contract collapsed (1989), Format International's
  bankruptcy (1988) and the police investigation, Tuvalu's output **appears to have dropped
  sharply** before IGPC-era volumes resumed. **The extent and exact years of this trough are
  Uncertain** — no source consulted gives a year-by-year issue count. Do not assert a
  zero-issue year without a catalogue check.
- **Post-2016 / post-2021:** Not a gap in issuing, but a **gap in coverage**. SG's Western
  Pacific 4th ed. stops at August 2016; StampWorld's Tuvalu run is described as 1976–2021.
  Recent Tuvalu material may be genuinely absent from accessible reference data.
- **No other multi-year gaps identified.** Tuvalu has issued more or less continuously since
  1976 — the criticism has always been of *excess*, never of silence.

## 7. Forgery risk and wallpaper risk

**This is the section that matters most, and the reputation needs to be split by era.**

### Forgery risk: LOW

No evidence was found of a significant classic-forgery problem for Tuvalu. There is no
Tuvalu equivalent of the Fournier/Sperati material. The risks that exist are **manufactured
varieties and fraudulent representation**, not counterfeit stamps:

- **The 1984–89 "deliberate errors" affair.** Philatelic Distribution Corporation is reported
  to have produced **c. 14,000 deliberate errors** — inverted centres, missing elements,
  perforation varieties — sold at inflated prices as though accidental. These are *genuine
  printings of fraudulent character*: real stamps, dishonestly marketed.
- **The 1992 trial.** Tuvalu's government sought injunctions in 1988; police raids followed;
  **eight men were arrested in 1990** and tried at **Southwark Crown Court, April–July 1992**.
  **All eight were acquitted.** The judge held that *producing* varieties was not itself
  unlawful — the offence lay only in falsely marketing them as accidental errors. Contracts
  had permitted reprints and varieties.
- **Practical consequence:** any Tuvalu imperforate, colour proof, "specimen", missing-colour
  or inverted-centre item from **1984–1989** should be treated as **presumptively deliberate
  agency product**, not a genuine production error, regardless of dealer description. This is
  the single highest-value provenance rule for Tuvalu.

### Wallpaper / agency risk: HIGH — but the reputation is era-specific and partly unearned

**Where it is fully deserved (1984–1988):**

- Tuvalu participated in the **"Leaders of the World" (LOW)** programme from **29 February 1984**
  (Locomotives Part 1). Reported LOW volume for Tuvalu in 1984 alone: **24 sets, 246 stamps.**
- Simultaneously, the **eight island administrations** issued ~100 stamps each, **~800 stamps
  in five years** for a country of a few thousand people. This is the textbook definition of
  issuing beyond postal need.
- **Gibbons acted on it editorially** — the island issues went to the **Appendix**, explicitly
  on grounds of excess issue and unavailability at face value. That is a documented catalogue
  sanction, and it is the strongest single piece of evidence that the wallpaper charge is
  substantiated for this period.
- The commercial collapse is itself evidence: **receivers were appointed at Philatelists on
  15 January 1987**, holding roughly **£20 million of unsold LOW stock**. The market had
  already rejected the material.

**Where it is NOT deserved (1976 – c. 1983):**

- The foundation period under **Crown Agents (1976–77)** and the early Philatelists years
  produced a modest, conventional Commonwealth new-issue programme. **The wallpaper label
  should not be applied to 1976–1983 Tuvalu.** If a dealer or database flags the 1976
  overprints or the late-1970s definitives as "wallpaper", that is lazy reputation-transfer.

**Where it is contested (IGPC era, c. 1990s – present):**

- Tuvalu is a documented **IGPC** client, and IGPC has drawn general criticism for
  "inappropriate and excessive issues". Scott numbers reaching ~1468 by 2020 imply sustained
  high-volume output.
- **However, no specific documented finding against Tuvalu's modern issues was located** —
  no catalogue delisting, no regulatory action, no named investigation. The charge against the
  modern era rests on volume and on IGPC's general reputation, not on a Tuvalu-specific
  determination.

### Named agencies: get these right

| Agency | Tuvalu client? | Period |
|---|---|---|
| Crown Agents | **Yes** | 1976–1977 |
| Philatelists Ltd / Philatelists (1980) Ltd | **Yes** | 1977–1987 |
| Philatelic Distribution Corporation (PDC) | **Yes** | 1987–1989 |
| Format International Security Printers (printer, not agency) | **Yes** | 1977–1988 |
| **IGPC** | **Yes** | c. 1990s – present (start date Uncertain) |
| **Stamperija** | **NO — MYTH** | Never. Stamperija's Pacific client was the **Solomon Islands** (from 2012). Tuvalu is repeatedly misattributed to Stamperija; sources consulted place Tuvalu firmly on IGPC's client list, not Stamperija's. |
| "Philatelic Collector Inc" | **Not found** | No source consulted connects this name to Tuvalu. Do not repeat it. |

### UPU illegal-issue circulars — an explicit negative finding

**No UPU circular naming Tuvalu was found.** This is a deliberate, checked negative, not an
omission:

- The Philatelic Webmasters Organization's review of **2017 WNS/UPU circulars** names
  **Croatia, Kiribati and Rwanda** — **not Tuvalu**. (Note the irony: Tuvalu's sibling
  successor state Kiribati *was* named; Tuvalu was not.)
- **Tuvalu does not participate in the WNS** (WADP Numbering System) — it is listed among
  IGPC clients absent from WNS alongside Aitutaki, Dominica, Gambia, Palau and Turkmenistan.
- **Critically: WNS absence is not evidence of illegality.** The same source argues this
  explicitly, and demonstrates the point with the Solomon Islands — 800+ stamps in 2015, fully
  Scott-listed, entirely absent from WNS. The WNS database is incomplete by design and by
  neglect.

**Therefore: do not state or imply that Tuvalu has been declared an illegal issuer by the UPU.**
The accurate statement is: *Tuvalu is absent from WNS and has been subject to catalogue
sanction by Gibbons for one specific 1984–88 series; no UPU illegal-issue circular naming
Tuvalu was located.*

### CTO and remainder material

- CTO Tuvalu material circulates (e.g. CTO examples of mid-1970s/80s issues are routinely
  offered), and the £20m of unsold LOW stock guarantees large remainder quantities entered the
  market from 1987 onward.
- **Uncertain:** whether Tuvalu ran a systematic bulk CTO programme comparable to the classic
  CTO issuers. No source consulted documents one. **Do not assert a CTO programme** — assert
  only that CTO examples and post-collapse remainders exist.

## 8. Image sourcing

- **Wikimedia Commons: effectively no usable Tuvalu stamp images.** No Tuvalu stamp category
  of substance was located, and Commons' country-specific stamp-copyright guidance has **no
  Tuvalu entry** — meaning uploads have no established PD tag to rely on.
- **Copyright position, layered and awkward:**
  1. Any work must be PD or freely licensed **in both Tuvalu and the United States** to be
     Commons-eligible.
  2. **Pre-independence material** (Gilbert & Ellice Islands, and arguably the 1976 overprints
     on 1971 G&E designs) may fall under **UK Crown copyright**, which for stamp designs
     published before 1 October 1969 ran **50 years** and has therefore expired for the older
     G&E issues. This is the **most promising PD route** — and it covers the *predecessor*
     country, not Tuvalu proper.
  3. **Post-1976 Tuvalu designs are the problem.** Tuvalu's own copyright term was not
     established in this research. Worse, designs from the agency periods were commissioned by
     and may be **owned by the agency** (Philatelists Ltd, PDC, IGPC) rather than by the
     Tuvaluan state — so even a generous reading of Tuvaluan government-works doctrine may not
     free them. **IGPC in particular asserts commercial rights over its designs.**
- **Practical sourcing options, none clean:**
  - Commercial databases (StampWorld, Colnect, Mystic, HipStamp, worldstampcompany) — good
    coverage, **not reusably licensed**.
  - `golowesstamps.com` Format-printers archive — unusually complete for Tuvalu 1977–88
    *including* the island issues, with Scott and SG numbers. Reference-grade; **licence
    unstated**.
  - Tuvalu Post Limited official site (`stamps.tuvalupost.tv`) — authoritative for modern
    issues; **licence unstated, presume all rights reserved**.
- **Recommendation:** treat Gilbert & Ellice pre-1970 as the only defensible reusable-image
  pool. For Tuvalu 1976+, assume images require licensing or fair-use justification and plan
  the ingest accordingly. Do not assume "small country = public domain".

## 9. Explicitly uncertain

Listed generously, as instructed. None of the following should be stated as fact without
further verification:

1. **Subject of the 1 January 1976 three-stamp commemorative set.** Not named in any source.
2. **Exact catalogue range of the 1976 overprint provisionals.** SG 1–15 / Scott 1–15 is
   plausible (matching a 15-value 1971 G&E series) but unverified.
3. **Printer of the 1976 overprints.** Format is excluded (started 1977); no positive
   attribution found.
4. **Date IGPC took on Tuvalu.** "Early 1990s" is plausible. Wikipedia's IGPC text can be
   misread as 1958, which is impossible.
5. **Highest current SG number for Tuvalu.** Unknown; SG coverage verified only to Aug 2016.
6. **Total Scott major numbers.** Only the Scott 1468 (2020) anchor is verified; the
   1,400–1,600 range is inferred.
7. **Colnect's Tuvalu count.** Site inaccessible.
8. **Exact per-island stamp counts for the eight island series.** "About 100 each" is the
   figure in circulation; not independently confirmed per island.
9. **Exact SG Appendix wording.** Reported via forum, not read from the catalogue.
10. **Inscription word order on island issues** (`TUVALU NUKUFETAU` vs `NUKUFETAU TUVALU`).
11. **Whether any Tuvalu year had zero issues** during the 1989–early-1990s trough.
12. **Whether Tuvalu operated a systematic CTO programme.** CTO items exist; a programme is
    not documented.
13. **Tuvalu's domestic copyright term** and whether stamp designs are government works.
14. **Whether the island issues were ever genuinely postally valid** in their own islands, or
    were philatelic-only from the outset. This is the crux of the Gibbons objection and was
    not resolved.
15. **Whether Scott has ever reduced or reviewed its listing** of the island issues in later
    editions. Only the "Scott gives full listing" position was confirmed, without an edition date.

### Myths flagged

- **MYTH: "Stamperija produced Tuvalu's stamps."** False. Tuvalu is IGPC; Stamperija's Pacific
  client was the Solomon Islands.
- **MYTH: "The UPU declared Tuvalu an illegal issuer."** No such circular was found.
  Kiribati appears in 2017 circulars; Tuvalu does not.
- **MYTH: "Scott and Gibbons refuse to list Tuvalu."** False as a blanket claim. Both list
  Tuvalu. Only the 1984–88 island issues are appendixed by Gibbons, and Scott lists even those.
- **MYTH: "Absence from WNS proves illegitimacy."** Explicitly rejected by the philatelic
  literature; the Solomon Islands counter-example is decisive.
- **MYTH: "Tuvalu has always been a wallpaper issuer."** False for 1976 – c. 1983.
- **MYTH: "Seven islands issued stamps."** Eight did. **Nanumaga** is the commonly dropped one.
  Niulakita never issued.
- **CAUTION:** AI-generated dealer pages fabricate Scott/SG numbers and printer attributions for
  small Pacific issuers. Treat any un-sourced catalogue number for Tuvalu island issues as
  suspect until checked against a physical catalogue.
